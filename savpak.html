Roy and the Rout: England
Demolish Australia by Eight Wickets
to Reach Their First Final Since 1992

For twenty-seven years England had lived with the memory of 1992 — Botham in full sail at Melbourne, Gooch at the top, a final that ended in defeat but that contained, at least, the dignity of participation. On a Thursday afternoon at Edgbaston, they earned the right to make a new memory. Chris Woakes and Jofra Archer dismantled Australia's top order for two hundred and twenty-three. Jason Roy and Jonny Bairstow produced one hundred and twenty-four in seventeen overs. England won by eight wickets with more than seventeen overs to spare. The defending champions went home. The hosts went to Lord's.

The five-time champions arrived at Edgbaston having beaten England by sixty-four runs at Lord's three weeks earlier, in a group stage match that had restored some of the competitive equilibrium between the sides — a reminder that the data showing England had won ten of their last twelve ODIs against Australia contained, within those two losses, evidence that the gap was not as wide as the dominant statistic implied. They arrived, also, with Aaron Finch in form, Steve Smith restored to international cricket after his ball-tampering suspension and batting with the accumulative patience that had always been his most important quality, and Mitchell Starc eighteen wickets into a tournament that seemed, at its beginning, as though it might end with a record. None of these qualities, in the end, proved sufficient. Chris Woakes took three wickets for twenty runs from eight overs, and what Australia had assembled between those wickets — two hundred and twenty-three — was a total that England's batsmen, in the condition they were in on this particular Thursday, were always going to consume without great exertion.

Australia batted with the specific difficulty that the Edgbaston surface presented: a pitch that offered movement to the seamers in the first fifteen overs, enough swing to justify the full length but not enough to make batting a matter of survival rather than judgement. Aaron Finch made zero — caught at third slip off Woakes from the third delivery of the innings, a ball that shaped away late and found the outside edge before the Australian captain had fully settled his footwork. David Warner made ten before an Archer delivery ended his World Cup in the manner of a judgment delivered without appeal. Peter Handscomb made four. And though Usman Khawaja played with admirable composure for forty-nine from sixty balls, and Alex Carey contributed thirty-six, what was absent was the long partnership that could push the total beyond the range England's batting had been routinely overcoming throughout the tournament.

Steve Smith's eighty-five from a hundred and nineteen deliveries was the innings that gave Australia's total its spine — the controlled accumulation of a batsman who, having been absent from international cricket for twelve months, has returned with the same qualities intact and the same limitations in place: extraordinary patience, extraordinary placement, a technique that makes every ball negotiable and every session potentially profitable. He drove cleanly through the covers, worked the ball through midwicket with his distinctive crouching front-foot play, and survived two dropped chances that, had they been held, might have reduced the match's second act to irrelevance before it had begun. But eighty-five from a hundred and nineteen was, on a day when the batting order around him contributed a collective sixty-eight runs from a hundred and seventy-six deliveries, an innings that was admirable without being adequate. Australia were all out in the forty-ninth over for two hundred and twenty-three, and the contest's trajectory was already, to anyone who had watched England bat in this tournament, apparent.

Roy, Bairstow and the Dismantling of a Target

Jason Roy had missed England's first five matches of this tournament with a hamstring injury. His return — against Bangladesh, and then against India — had coincided with England finding the form that had been promised but occasionally absent in the group stage. At Edgbaston on the eleventh of July, with Australia's two hundred and twenty-three in front of them and a Lord's final three days away, Roy batted as if the entire summer had been leading to this specific afternoon and as if the specific afternoon required, above all else, the application of maximum force to minimum caution. His eighty-five from sixty-five deliveries — four fours, five sixes, the innings of a batsman who has identified the target and the conditions and concluded that the appropriate response is the one that removes all doubt about the outcome as quickly as possible — was the innings that made the afternoon feel, from roughly the tenth over, like a procession rather than a contest.

The opening partnership between Roy and Jonny Bairstow — one hundred and twenty-four runs from seventeen overs — was one of those passages of ODI batting that seems, in real time, to be redefining the category. They drove Mitchell Starc through the covers with the full, high-elbow extension of batsmen who have decided that a bowler of this quality deserves no particular respect from the off side. They pulled Pat Cummins through midwicket with the flat-bat certainty of batsmen who have read the length early enough to play the shot they intended rather than the shot the ball suggested. They ran between the wickets with the crisp efficiency of a partnership that has batted together long enough to know, by instinct, whether the second run is there before the first has been completed. And when Roy was finally dismissed — caught at mid-on off Jason Behrendorff, the ball struck with all his force but finding the fielder's hands rather than the gap — England needed only ninety-nine more from thirty-three overs with eight wickets in hand, a calculation that was, in any practical sense, already concluded.

"Absolutely delighted. Edgbaston has been very kind to us. I was six in 1992. I don't remember much. But I've seen the highlights a lot. Sunday is a massive opportunity."

Eoin Morgan, England captain, post-match at Edgbaston

Joe Root made forty-nine not out from forty-six deliveries in a partnership with Morgan that was technically unbroken, Morgan hitting the winning four off Behrendorff in the thirty-second over to complete an eight-wicket victory with a hundred and seven balls remaining. England's win was not merely comprehensive in its margin; it was comprehensive in its quality — the bowling disciplined and probing, the fielding sharp, the batting from the moment Roy arrived at the crease entirely in command of the match's terms. They had beaten the defending champions by eight wickets in a semi-final, for the first time since 1992 they were in a World Cup final, and the machine built in the years since the humiliation of the 2015 tournament had, at last, delivered its most important result.

Australia's defeat carried within it the residue of a campaign that had, in most respects, been excellent — five wins from nine group stage matches, a net run rate that had comfortably secured their semi-final place, Steve Smith's return and David Warner's consistent contributions throughout. What it lacked was the depth of batting that England possessed and the specific adaptability — the willingness to play differently in different conditions — that the surface at Edgbaston demanded on the eleventh of July. When Woakes removed Finch in the third delivery and Archer removed Warner in the fourteenth over, Australia's top order had provided seven runs from two wickets and the match's shape was already visible. They could not recover it. England, with the confidence of a side that has spent four years building towards exactly this kind of day, required only thirty-two overs to settle it.

Haris Sohail's Blitz at Lord's:
Pakistan Knock South Africa
Out of the World Cup

There is a category of innings that announces itself not through technique or patience but through pure, unfiltered intent — the innings of a man who has been given his chance late and has resolved, silently and irrevocably, to make certain it is not wasted. Haris Sohail had played five one-day internationals before this World Cup. At Lord's on the twenty-third of June, batting at number five with Pakistan needing acceleration and the tournament's highest-profile ground providing its most hallowed occasion, he scored eighty-nine from fifty-nine deliveries. Pakistan made three hundred and eight. South Africa's World Cup, which had been failing quietly for six weeks, ended at Lord's with two hundred and fifty-nine on the board and the mathematics finally, irrevocably, closed.

Lord's had not hosted a match in this World Cup until the twenty-third of June, which lent the occasion a particular gravity — the first time this tournament had arrived at the ground that regards itself, with the quiet certainty of an institution that has never needed to make the case aloud, as the home of cricket. Both teams arrived at it in states of considerable fragility. South Africa had won one match from six; Pakistan had won two from five, and required, at a minimum, a victory here and then further victories in their remaining fixtures alongside favourable results elsewhere to maintain any credible claim to a semi-final place. For Pakistan, Lord's was the beginning of a possible revival or the definitive end of a disappointing campaign. For South Africa, it was, in the coldest reading of the mathematics, the last match they could afford to lose. They lost it by forty-nine runs.

Pakistan's innings was, in its first twenty-five overs, a moderately cautious accumulation that offered nothing to predict its conclusion. Fakhar Zaman and Imam-ul-Haq opened with a fifty partnership in a powerplay that asked more questions of the bowling than the batting — both moving the ball around the field with the busy efficiency of openers who have found their partnership rhythm. Imam made thirty-six before edging Lungi Ngidi behind, and Fakhar's thirty extended the opening stand to fifty-eight before Kagiso Rabada produced a delivery of full, late swing that beat the outside edge and trimmed the off stump. The surface at Lord's, which offers the seam bowlers their rewards in the first ten overs and then tends to quicken and flatten for the batting side, had given South Africa their two early wickets. Pakistan, at eighty-one for two in the fifteenth over, needed to convert the platform into something substantial. Babar Azam began the conversion.

Babar's Architecture and Haris's Explosion

Babar Azam made sixty-nine from eighty deliveries — the innings that gave Pakistan's middle overs their framework without providing them their shape. He drove through the off side with the high elbow and complete follow-through that has always been the most immediately visible quality of his batting, worked the ball through midwicket off his legs with the wristiness of a batsman who has spent enough time studying the craft of placement to make boundaries from deliveries that would, to most batsmen, produce only a single. He and Fakhar had shared fifty-eight for the first wicket; he and Mohammad Hafeez put on sixty-two for the third. When Rabada removed him in the thirty-first over — caught at mid-on driving a delivery that was fuller than Babar had anticipated — Pakistan were at one hundred and sixty-six for four, and the last nineteen overs required the sort of explosive contribution that their batting order had, in earlier matches of this tournament, occasionally failed to produce at the critical moment. Haris Sohail, who had not played a single group match before this one, produced it instead.

Haris's eighty-nine from fifty-nine deliveries — nine fours and three sixes, the second-highest score by a Pakistani number five or below in World Cup history behind Imran Khan's unbeaten hundred against Sri Lanka in 1983 — was an innings of such compressed violence that it altered the match's terms within five overs of its beginning. He drove Ngidi through the covers with the full extension of a left-hander who has been waiting for this pitch and this moment and this particular invitation to play the shot he prefers above all others. He swept Imran Tahir over the deep square leg boundary with the decisive flat-bat authority of a batsman who has identified the line, committed to the shot, and discovered that the timing is precisely what he imagined it would be. He pull-drove Chris Morris high and straight, the ball clearing the boundary boards above the media centre with the specific trajectory that separates the six that scrapes over the rope from the six that leaves no doubt. Pakistan's total moved from one hundred and sixty-six for four to two hundred and sixty-eight in the space of thirteen overs, and Wahab Riaz's late cameo of twenty-two pushed them past three hundred.

"A complete team performance. Haris batted like Jos Buttler. He came in and changed the game completely. Sometimes, change is good."

Sarfaraz Ahmed, Pakistan captain, post-match at Lord's

South Africa's Elimination: A Chase Without Conviction

South Africa needed three hundred and nine. There was, among the professionals covering this tournament, a broad consensus that the target was beyond a batting order that had failed, in five of their six previous matches, to produce the collective sustained batting performance that such totals require. What South Africa produced — two hundred and fifty-nine for nine in their fifty overs — was neither as bad as their worst day in this tournament nor as good as their batting order's quality warranted, and the gap between those two assessments is where the match's story resides. Hashim Amla, their most experienced opener and the batsman around whom their chase had to be built, fell for two in the second over — Wahab Riaz finding his outside edge with a delivery of full, late swing that produced a thick edge to Fakhar at gully. The wicket arrived before South Africa had begun, and it confirmed something that the scoreboard had been saying softly for six weeks: that this particular team, in this particular tournament, were fragile in precisely the ways their opponents had identified.

Quinton de Kock made forty-seven with the attacking intent of a left-hander who has recognised that his team's best chance of chasing three hundred and nine is to establish an early rate rather than preserve wickets for a push in the final overs. He drove Mohammad Amir through the covers with the clean contact of a natural timer, swept Shadab Khan over deep square leg with the decisive flat-bat authority that is his most dangerous stroke against spin, and rotated the strike with the crisp efficiency of a batsman who understands that boundaries alone do not chase totals of this size. When Shadab removed him in the nineteenth over — a back-of-the-hand googly that beat the slog-sweep and disturbed the stumps — South Africa were at ninety-one for two, and the partnership that Faf du Plessis was now required to build with whoever presented themselves at number four had to sustain not merely the scoreboard but the belief that the match was winnable. It is a different quality from batting; it is harder to produce on demand.

Faf du Plessis made sixty-three from seventy-nine deliveries, and it was, in its careful construction and its ultimate insufficiency, the perfect summary of South Africa's 2019 World Cup campaign. He drove beautifully through the off side, worked the ball through midwicket with the left-handed elegance that has always been his most distinctive batting quality, and refused, for the entire duration of his innings, to accelerate at a rate that would have made the equation manageable. He was caught behind off Amir in the thirtieth over, top-edging a heave to Sarfaraz — the wicket that reduced South Africa to one hundred and thirty-six for four and ended any realistic possibility of the chase being completed. Wahab Riaz took three wickets for forty-six, Shadab three for fifty. Pakistan won by forty-nine runs. South Africa were eliminated.

South Africa left Lord's having played their last match of the 2019 World Cup with the particular dignity of a team that has, at the end, produced its best cricket of the tournament too late for it to matter. JP Duminy and Imran Tahir, both playing their final ODIs for South Africa, received the acknowledgement that careers of their quality deserved, the crowd at Lord's responding with the generosity it extends to cricketers of long service and proven excellence. What they were also acknowledging, though the mathematics are too blunt for the affections of the moment, is that South Africa's 2019 World Cup — five defeats from seven matches, the early exits of their best batsmen in match after match, the bowling never quite sharp enough to compensate for the batting's failures — had been a campaign of consistent underperformance from a side whose talent should have produced something better. The Mecca of Cricket had hosted their last rites. The game moved on without them.
