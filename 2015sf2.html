The Summer of Steve
Continues: Smith's Hundred
Ends India's Unbeaten Run

Four months earlier, India had arrived in Australia holding the Border-Gavaskar Trophy and the World Cup simultaneously — a dual custody of cricket's most consequential silverware unprecedented in the game's recent history. Then they ran into Steven Smith. He had tormented them with a century in every Test of that summer, and at the Sydney Cricket Ground on this floodlit Thursday evening, playing in the World Cup semi-final with India's eleven-match unbeaten tournament run on the line, he arrived again: the same concentrated stillness, the same unorthodox trigger-movement, the same end result. A hundred. And Australia, for the seventh time in seven semi-finals, in the tournament for which they are the most permanent fixture, advanced to the final.

The Sydney Cricket Ground at night acquires a quality that its daytime aspect does not quite possess — the floodlights turning the outfield a shade of green too intense for an afternoon in any season, the crowd's noise sharpened by the specific acoustics of a ground whose members' pavilion and Victor Trumper Stand enclose the sound rather than releasing it into the open air. On this Thursday in late March 2015, with the stands carrying the blue and the saffron of two cricketing civilisations in rough proportion to their populations' relative sizes — India's diaspora filling the SCG in numbers sufficient to make the match feel, as Eoin Morgan had observed of Pakistan supporters at Trent Bridge four years hence, like an away fixture for the home side — Australia batted with the controlled authority of a team that has won six World Cup semi-finals from six and has learned, through those six, that the stage is not the enemy of the performance but its amplifier. They posted 328 for 7. India, for all the quality and depth of their batting order and all the pedigree of their recent form, made 233. The margin was 95 runs, and it was, in its final character, a margin of authority rather than fortune: the score of a match in which the better team on the day had demonstrated their superiority across both innings with the kind of completeness that leaves very little room for debate.

Michael Clarke, Australia's captain and the most senior presence in a batting order that had, across this World Cup, been at times brilliant and at times inconsistent, won the toss and batted on a Sydney surface that offered pace and carry to the fast bowlers early but was expected to flatten into something more comfortable as the day-night match progressed and the dew began to settle on the outfield. David Warner, batting at the top of the order with the aggressive intentionality that had made him, in the preceding years, among the most feared openers in the world game, lasted three overs before Umesh Yadav — the quickest bowler in India's attack and the only one capable of genuinely hurrying the Australian batsmen on a surface of this pace — found the outside edge of a drive that was a fraction wider than Warner's arms were comfortable accommodating. Kohli, stationed at cover, held the catch cleanly. Fifteen for one. The early wicket had given India the morning's momentum, and for a single over it appeared possible that Yadav and the accompanying pace of Mohammed Shami might press the advantage into genuine discomfort. Steven Smith walked out to bat at number three, and the possibility dissolved.

Smith's Hundred: The Finest Hour of a Summer Already Defined by Excellence

The summer that Steven Smith had constructed across the four preceding months of Australia's home season had been, in its statistical totality, one of the most dominant individual contributions to a bilateral series that Australian cricket had witnessed in the post-Bradman era. He had made centuries in the first three Tests against India. He had made them with a technique so deliberately unorthodox — the bat raised high and late, the body open at the point of impact, the wrists turning the ball through the leg side from deliveries that the coaching manual would describe as outside off stump and therefore unsuitable for the shot — that commentators had repeatedly raised the question of whether a technique so apparently improvised could be sustained at the highest level under the most sustained and targeted pressure. The answer Smith had provided, over the preceding four months and across many variations of the Indian bowling attack's attempt to dismiss him, was consistent: it could. It could not merely be sustained but refined, adjusted, and deployed with the growing authority of a batsman who had begun to understand that his idiosyncrasy was not a flaw in his technique but the source of his particular superiority.

He made 105 from 93 balls at the SCG. His fifty came from 53 balls — a rate of careful accumulation that reflected the early difficulty of the Sydney surface and the quality of Yadav's opening spell, which had been disciplined enough to restrict the Australian batsmen to singles and twos rather than boundaries. He drove cleanly through the off side, pulled the short ball with the assured weight-transfer of a batsman who has studied where the ball will arrive and is in position before it gets there, and accumulated through the middle overs with the specific quality that had defined his summer: total concentration, total commitment to each delivery, and the slightly uncanny capacity to find runs from balls that other batsmen would accept as dots. His century arrived in the thirty-fourth over from 88 balls, struck with a drive through the covers off Yadav that bisected the field perfectly and reached the boundary before the fielder had taken a step toward it. He raised his bat without extravagance — a measured, almost private acknowledgement, as though the century were confirmation of something he had already known rather than a surprise that required public expression. He was dismissed six balls later, for exactly 105, caught by Dhawan at mid-wicket off Yadav — a short ball, the pull shot fractionally mistimed, the ball carrying to the fielder at a height requiring a jump and a two-handed grab. Yadav had, in the end, found the method. It had required a hundred first.

"This has been a special summer for me personally. But tonight wasn't about me — it was about getting Australia to the final and doing whatever it took to make that happen."

Steven Smith, Man of the Match, post-match at Sydney Cricket Ground

Finch's Backbone and the Late Acceleration

Aaron Finch had come into this semi-final under modest scrutiny — his form across the group stage had been acceptable without being dominant, and the question of whether he was Australia's best available opener in ODI cricket had been raised, not urgently but persistently, in the preceding weeks. He answered it at the SCG with 81 from 116 balls — the innings not of a batsman under pressure but of a batsman performing his function with the workmanlike thoroughness of a man who understands that his role is to provide the platform and that the platform does not require ornament. He drove cleanly when the length was full. He was organised against the short ball. He accumulated at a rate that, combined with Smith's aggression at the other end, produced 182 for the second wicket across 124 balls — the partnership that gave Australia's total its architecture. Finch was dismissed in the thirty-eighth over, caught by Dhawan at short midwicket off Yadav for 81 — a short ball that he pulled fractionally too early, the top-edge rising and then settling into Dhawan's hands. He had done what was required.

Mitchell Johnson's contribution of 27 from 9 balls — three consecutive sixes in one over off Shami, the last a pulled shot struck with the specific, violent efficiency of a man who has hit a hundred sixes and knows exactly how to hit the hundred-and-first — moved Australia from a total of 248 at the death to something altogether more demanding. Glenn Maxwell contributed 23, Clarke a composed 19, and Faulkner 21. Three hundred and twenty-eight was the highest total in a World Cup semi-final, and it felt, in the warm Sydney evening with the dew beginning to settle on the outfield, like a total of genuine menace: not so large as to be immediately dispiriting for India, but large enough that a chase would require their very best batting across all ten wickets rather than heroics from three or four.

Johnson and the Two Critical Wickets That Killed the Chase

India's reply began with the particular optimism that a well-set batting order always carries into a large chase — the optimism of a side that has been here before, that has chased down targets of 330 and 329 and knows the mechanical requirements of the task. Shikhar Dhawan and Rohit Sharma, India's most experienced and most reliable ODI opening combination, added 76 in 12.5 overs — a partnership of sensible, purposeful accumulation, neither batsman attempting to manufacture the run rate before the conditions demanded it, both of them scoring at a pace that kept India slightly behind the required rate but not disastrously so. Dhawan made 45, driving cleanly through the covers and pulling the short ball with the flat-bat authority that had, across this entire tournament, been his most reliable scoring stroke. He was caught at deep extra cover off Hazlewood — a full delivery that Dhawan had attempted to drive aerially and had sent instead to the fielder rather than over him. Seventy-six for one in the thirteenth over. The partnership was over. What came next was the moment on which the entire match turned.

Virat Kohli, India's best batsman and their most ferociously competitive individual performer, arrived at the crease with India needing to convert the opening stand's momentum into something sustained. He faced Mitchell Johnson. The first ball he received was a short ball of 140 kilometres per hour, angled into his body, rising sharply from a length that gave him neither the time nor the room to play it safely. Kohli attempted a top-edged hook — the shot of a batsman who has been surprised by the pace and has committed to an aggressive response out of reflex rather than calculation. The ball carried to Brad Haddin behind the stumps, who took it with the clean certainty of a wicketkeeper who has been in this position many times before. Kohli was out for one. He shook his head and walked back to the pavilion with the tight, slightly self-reproachful expression of a batsman who knows he has gifted a wicket at exactly the wrong moment. Two balls later, Rohit Sharma — who had been batting with the watchful solidity of a man who had identified the situation correctly and was playing accordingly — was bowled by Johnson for 34, a delivery of late in-swing that found the gap between bat and front pad and hit middle stump. India were 78 for 3 in the fifteenth over, having lost Kohli and Sharma in three balls. The chase, which had been challenging before these dismissals, now required something that Indian cricket's remaining resources would find difficult to provide.

Suresh Raina and Ajinkya Rahane both made starts without converting them. Raina's 65 from 57 balls was the innings of a man at the lower end of his form — technically disciplined, periodically forceful, ultimately insufficient. He and Rahane put on 52 for the fifth wicket before Faulkner found Rahane's outside edge and the partnership ended at 178. MS Dhoni arrived at the crease in the thirty-sixth over with India at 178 for 5, needing 151 from eighty-five balls — an equation requiring, at its most optimistic, a sustained period of Dhoni at his most imperious. What Dhoni produced was, characteristically, more composed than imperial: 65 from 65 balls, an innings of determined accumulation that kept the scoreboard moving and the match's theoretical possibility alive through the fortieth over, before Steven Smith — at point, with one stump to aim at, fielding a Dhoni flick to mid-wicket — threw direct and caught Dhoni marginally short of his crease. Dhoni run out for 65. Maxwell's direct hit was the dismissal that ended whatever hope remained.

Yadav's Four Wickets and the Quiet Consolation of Quality

Umesh Yadav had, across this match, been India's most persistent and most dangerous bowling threat — a right-arm fast bowler of genuine pace who had, over the course of ten overs, claimed five wickets including the match's most important batsman for exactly his century. His four for 72 was the bowling performance of the match from India's perspective and, in the manner of great losing bowling performances, it carried within it both the satisfaction of execution and the specific, contained frustration of execution that was insufficient for the situation. He had bowled Smith for 105 and Warner for 12 and Finch for 81 and Faulkner for 21. He had generated the pace that had, at various points in Australia's innings, made the batsmen uncomfortable and required adaptations of technique that other bowlers had not demanded. He was not enough, because 328 was not a total that four wickets from one bowler could prevent, and because India's own batting had not provided the chase with the sustained quality it required.

James Faulkner — the man who had delivered the decisive bowling spell — finished with three for 59, bowling at the death with the left-arm variations that had proven, across this World Cup, the most reliably effective set of variations in Australia's attack. He bowled Ashwin and Mohit Sharma from consecutive deliveries in the forty-sixth over, and Starc finished the innings in the forty-seventh with a yorker that rattled Yadav's stumps while the SCG's fireworks system detonated over the stands. Australia had won by 95 runs. They would meet New Zealand at the Melbourne Cricket Ground in three days for the right to be called world champions. They were, in reaching the final, extending to seven a semi-final winning record that had no precedent in the tournament's history and that spoke, more eloquently than any individual match summary could, about the systematic, institutional quality of Australian World Cup cricket across thirty years of the competition's history.

India boarded their flights the following day carrying the specific weight that defending champions carry when they depart a tournament earlier than the tournament expected them to — the weight of unmet expectation, of an eleven-match unbeaten run that had become, in the popular imagination, the outline of a coronation rather than the qualified achievement of a very good team doing very well. They had been beaten by a better side, on this particular evening, at this particular ground: Smith's hundred had settled the match's terms by the thirty-fifth over, and Johnson's two wickets in three balls had terminated the chase before it had properly established its character. Dhoni, the last man of consequence at the crease, had made 65 and had been run out by a throw of such precision and such speed that it had the quality of a full stop placed with deliberate authority. India were gone. Australia remained. The MCG awaited. And Steven Smith, who had spent the southern hemisphere summer making hundreds against this batting order, had made one more, in the largest match he had played to that point, and had ensured that the summer of Steve had one more chapter to write.
