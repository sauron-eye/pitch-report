The IPL has ruined Test cricket averages and I will not apologise for saying so.

The statistical record of Test cricket — a document assembled over a hundred and forty-seven years of the most rigorous examination the game has ever devised — is being quietly distorted by batsmen trained to a different standard. The numbers no longer mean what they used to mean.

By Muhammad Hamza Cricket Writer, The Pitch Report The Pitch Report

There is a particular kind of dishonesty in the contemporary cricket conversation that involves the pretence that numbers mean the same thing they always meant, even when the context in which those numbers are produced has changed so fundamentally that comparison has become, if not impossible, then certainly dishonest in a way that rewards examination. The batting average in Test cricket — that most venerable of statistics, the metric by which we have compared Jack Hobbs and Don Bradman and Sachin Tendulkar and Brian Lara across the full arc of the game's history — is being inflated, in the current era, by a generation of batsmen whose formative cricket was played in the Indian Premier League, and whose instincts, under pressure in Test matches, reflect that formation in ways that the averages do not capture and the statistical record does not acknowledge. I am going to say this plainly, because it is a thing that needs saying plainly: the IPL has changed what a Test batting average means, and anyone who tells you otherwise has not looked at the numbers carefully enough.

Let me begin with the mechanism, because the argument is sometimes dismissed as sentiment dressed as analysis, and it is not. The IPL rewards a specific set of batting behaviours: the ability to strike the ball hard and high in the early overs, when field restrictions and bowling attacks deployed in short spells reward aggression; the willingness to attempt the aerial route over the infield against deliveries that, in Test match conditions, would be defended; the mental state of the batsman who knows he has twenty deliveries to score thirty runs and that the consequence of dismissal in the fifteenth over is minor — because there are ten more wickets coming and the target is already in reach. These behaviours, practised across six weeks every year for fifteen years, produce instincts. Instincts are not turned off when the format changes. They persist, modify slightly, and express themselves in the moments of pressure when the trained response overrides the intended response.

What a Batting Average Is Actually Measuring

The Test batting average is a measure of a batsman's ability to score runs without getting out. At its most sophisticated, it is a measure of a batsman's ability to make the judgement, delivered at sixty-eight miles per hour or higher, about which balls merit attack and which merit defence, and to make that judgement correctly across a large enough sample of deliveries to constitute a genuine result rather than a fortunate run. The great Test averages — Bradman's ninety-nine, Sutcliffe's sixty-six, Hutton's fifty-six, Tendulkar's fifty-three across two hundred and forty-four matches — were constructed across careers in which Test cricket was the highest available expression of the batting craft, in which the instincts formed in first-class cricket were calibrated specifically for the demands of the long game, and in which the batsman who played an expansive drive at a ball on a good length on day three of a Test match was regarded as having made a technical error, not a commercially viable risk assessment.

The modern batsman who averages fifty in Test cricket has, in many cases, achieved that average through a combination of genuine Test match quality and a frequency of high scores in flatter conditions against weaker bowling attacks that the schedule, biased towards bilateral series between unequal nations, makes available more regularly than it did in the era of Hutton or Sobers. This is not the batsman's fault. The schedule is what it is, and the batsman plays the cricket that is offered. But when we place his fifty alongside Hobbs's fifty-six and imply that the numbers are measuring the same quality of achievement, we are being imprecise in a way that the statisticians know to be imprecise and rarely acknowledge publicly, because acknowledging it inconveniences the narrative.

The IPL does not produce bad batsmen. It produces batsmen optimised for a different game — and when those batsmen carry their optimisations into Test cricket, the averages record the result without noting the distortion.

The Strike-Rate Contamination

The more subtle and, I would argue, more significant effect of the IPL on Test cricket batting is not the direct importation of T20 techniques into the five-day game but the contamination of judgment about what constitutes an acceptable batting tempo in Tests. There is a generation of batsmen now playing Test cricket who have been told, explicitly and implicitly, by coaches, by selectors, by the analytics departments of franchise teams, that a strike rate below a hundred in limited-overs cricket is a problem to be solved. That framing — strike rate as a performance metric to be maximised rather than as a context-dependent variable — migrates, inevitably, into the way those batsmen think about their role in Test matches, where the relevant metric is not strike rate but survival and accumulation. The consequence is a category of dismissal that did not exist in the same frequency before the franchise era: the batsman who is out for thirty or forty in a Test match not because he played a technically deficient shot but because he played a shot that his T20 instincts categorised as low-risk and his Test match judgment — had it been fully formed in the five-day format — would have categorised as unnecessary.

The averages record these dismissals as failures, which they are. What they do not record is the origin of those failures in a formation that the cricket economy now requires players to undergo. A young Indian batsman who wants a career in professional cricket in 2024 must play the IPL. He will play more IPL cricket across the formative years of his career than he will play first-class cricket. His instincts — the responses that operate below the level of conscious decision-making, the ones that determine how he plays in the forty-second over of a Test innings when he is on sixty and the match situation requires him to either consolidate or accelerate — will be shaped by the IPL. The Test average that results from this formation is measuring something real: his performance in Test cricket. What it is not measuring, and what it cannot measure without additional context, is whether that performance reflects his maximum possible quality in the five-day format, or whether it reflects the ceiling imposed by a formation that optimised for something else.

The Comparison Problem

The practical consequence of all of this is that cross-era comparison — the activity that cricket statistics exist primarily to facilitate, the reason we care what Bradman averaged and what Tendulkar averaged and what Williamson averages — has become, in the IPL era, significantly less meaningful than it was. When someone tells me that a current India batsman averages fifty-two in Test cricket and is therefore comparable to Sunil Gavaskar, who averaged fifty-one, I am being asked to accept that two numbers produced in radically different contexts, by batsmen formed in radically different ways, are measuring the same quality of achievement. They are not. Gavaskar was formed entirely in first-class and Test cricket. His instincts were calibrated, across every significant cricket he played from the age of sixteen, for the demands of the long game. The current batsman averaging fifty-two has been formed, in part, by six weeks every year of the most T20-specific cricket available. His fifty-two is real. It is not the same fifty-two.

I am aware that this argument can be taken too far. The IPL has also produced batsmen of extraordinary quality who have adapted to Test cricket with complete success — Virat Kohli is the example most frequently cited, and it is a fair one; Kohli's Test record across the period from 2014 to 2019 is as authentic a measure of batting excellence as any in the game's history, and it was produced by a man who had played IPL cricket every year of his career. The counter-example exists and is compelling. What it does not do is invalidate the general argument. Kohli is the exception. The pattern I am describing is the rule. And the rule is that the statistical record of Test cricket, which was designed to measure a batsman's quality in the most demanding conditions available, now contains a significant proportion of numbers produced in conditions distorted, at the level of formation and instinct, by a format that demands something different. We should say so. We should not apologise for saying so. The integrity of the historical record requires it.

None of this is the IPL's fault. The IPL is a commercial enterprise of extraordinary success, and it has produced cricket of genuine and spectacular quality, and it has brought the game to audiences that Test cricket would never have reached, and it has made the players who participate in it financially secure in a way that their predecessors were not. These are all real goods, and they deserve acknowledgment. The question is not whether the IPL is good or bad — it is both — but whether we are being honest about what it has done to the statistical record that the rest of cricket's history depends on for its continuity and its meaning. My argument is that we are not being honest about this, that the averages are being cited as though the context in which they were produced is irrelevant, and that the historians of the game will, in fifty years, look back at the comparative statistics of this era and note — with the benefit of the distance that we do not currently possess — that something changed around 2008 that the numbers, as recorded, do not reflect. The least we can do is begin acknowledging it now.
