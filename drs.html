Why the Decision Review System has made cricket more human, not less.

The critics say it has replaced human judgment with technology, interrupted the flow of the game, and produced outcomes that feel less certain rather than more. They are wrong on all three counts. The DRS has done something more interesting than they give it credit for — it has redistributed the game's humanity rather than removed it.

By Muhammad Hamza Technology Editor, The Pitch Report The Pitch Report

The argument against the Decision Review System has always had the structure of a nostalgic lament rather than a technical objection. At its core, the argument is this: before DRS, the umpire's decision was final, the game moved on, and the participants accepted the outcome as one of the conditions of play — the way they accepted bad light, damp outfields, and the movement that a ball develops on its sixty-seventh over. There was something in this acceptance that belonged to the game's character, that was part of what it meant to play cricket at any level: the acknowledgment that human judgment is fallible, that mistakes will be made, and that a team's ability to absorb those mistakes and maintain its composure was itself a part of the cricket being tested. The DRS, the argument runs, has replaced this acceptance with a technological appeal process that interrupts the game, undermines the authority of the on-field umpire, and produces outcomes that — because they are mediated by ball-tracking software and hotspot and Snicko — feel less resolved than the decisions they replaced. I understand the argument. I have made versions of it myself. I no longer believe it, and I want to explain why.

The first thing to understand about the Decision Review System is what it was designed to correct. It was not designed to replace human judgment with technological judgment. It was designed to correct a specific and well-documented category of error — the decision made by an on-field umpire who does not have the visual information necessary to make it correctly. The lbw decision, where the umpire is required to determine whether a ball that hits the pad would have gone on to hit the stumps, is the paradigm case. The umpire has one view, one moment, and no access to the trajectory of the ball after it makes contact with the batsman's pad. Ball-tracking technology has access to all of these things. The question is not whether the technology knows more than the umpire — it does, in this specific domain — but whether cricket wishes to use the information that the technology provides to make decisions that are more often correct. Framed in those terms, the argument against DRS becomes very difficult to make without appearing to prefer incorrect decisions to correct ones, which is a position that no one, stated plainly, is willing to defend.

The Umpire's Call Controversy and What It Actually Shows

The aspect of the DRS that has generated the most sustained criticism is the umpire's call — the zone around the edge of the stumps in which, even when ball-tracking suggests the ball would have hit the stumps, the original on-field decision is upheld. Critics of this element argue that it represents a half-measure: if the technology is good enough to be trusted for decisions where the ball would clearly hit the stumps, it should be trusted for decisions where the ball would barely hit them. The umpire's call zone, in this reading, is an institutional fudge — a way of maintaining the fiction of the umpire's authority in cases where the technology has already rendered that authority irrelevant.

I think this reading, while coherent, misunderstands the purpose of umpire's call and what it reflects about the genuine limitations of ball-tracking technology. The technology models the trajectory of the ball after the point of pad impact by extrapolating from its pre-impact trajectory, which is determined by speed, seam position, angle of delivery, and various aerodynamic factors that the software attempts to account for. This modelling is accurate to within a margin of error that the system's manufacturers quantify as approximately a centimetre and a half over the distance from pad impact to stump. This margin of error is real. It is not a political concession to the umpires' association. It is an honest acknowledgment that ball-tracking, however sophisticated, is not an infallible oracle — it is a probabilistic model, and probabilistic models have error margins, and when the margin of error of the model is larger than the margin by which the ball would miss or hit the stumps, the decision falls within the zone of genuine uncertainty. Umpire's call preserves the original decision in precisely those cases. That is not a flaw. That is the system correctly representing its own epistemic limitations.

Before DRS, a batsman dismissed by an umpiring error had no recourse and no remedy. He walked off knowing the decision was wrong and accepting it as the price of play. DRS did not remove that situation — but it reduced it to the cases where the technology genuinely cannot tell. That is a moral improvement, not merely a technical one.

The Drama It Has Added, Not Removed

The second argument against DRS — that it interrupts the flow and drama of cricket — is the one that I find most curious, because it runs so completely counter to the observable evidence. The DRS review has become, in the years since its widespread adoption, one of the most dramatically charged moments in Test cricket. The captain's conference — the fielding captain approaching the bowler, the brief discussion about whether the appeal has merit, the decision to review or not to review, conducted under time pressure and in full view of the crowd — is a moment of pure dramatic tension that did not exist before the system was introduced. The audience watching, whether at the ground or on television, is invited into a process of collective assessment — is it out? Was there an edge? Will the ball tracking show clipping the top of off stump? — that makes them participants in the decision rather than passive witnesses to it. The wait for the television umpire's review, the slow-motion replay, the ball-tracking projection: these are not interruptions to the drama. They are drama, of a specific and distinctive kind that the format has produced and that spectators have, demonstrably and enthusiastically, embraced.

Consider the strategic dimension that DRS has added to Test cricket captaincy. Before the system, the captain's role in relation to umpiring decisions was entirely passive: accept the decision, move on. The DRS has given the captain two reviews per innings — a scarce resource to be allocated wisely across fifty overs of bowling — and the management of those reviews has become a genuine dimension of Test match captaincy that rewards intelligence and penalises poor judgment. A captain who burns both reviews on speculative appeals in the first session and then watches a clear nick go undetected in the forty-first over because he has no reviews remaining has made a tactical error as real and as consequential as a bowling change made at the wrong moment. A captain who conserves his reviews, makes good decisions about when to deploy them, and uses the system's evidence correctly to save wickets that the on-field umpire would otherwise have wrongly given has gained a genuine competitive advantage. This is not the introduction of technology into cricket. It is the introduction of a new strategic variable that rewards the qualities — intelligence, restraint, good judgment under pressure — that cricket has always claimed to reward. The DRS, in this sense, is more cricket, not less.

The Human Story It Has Told

There is a final argument for the DRS that I find, in some ways, the most persuasive, and it is the least technical. The DRS has made the human beings involved in cricket — the players, the captains, the umpires — more visible in their fallibility and their excellence than they were before. Before the system, an umpire who made a wrong decision was simply an umpire who made a wrong decision: no more visible, no more memorable, than the thousands of correct decisions that preceded and followed it. After the system, when a decision is reviewed and overturned, the on-field umpire's error is documented, examined, and discussed — which might seem like an argument against the system from the umpires' perspective, and sometimes is made as one — but which has, over time, produced something unexpected: a deeper appreciation of what the on-field umpire is doing correctly. When you watch the ball-tracking on a review and see that the ball would have clipped the top of off stump, and then learn that the on-field umpire, standing at the bowler's end with a single view of the ball's trajectory, also said out — that judgment, made without access to the technology, is revealed as the achievement it is. The DRS, by showing us the technology's view, has made the umpire's judgment visible in a way that our previous acceptance of its finality never permitted.

What the critics of the system really want, I think, is not the elimination of technology from cricket but the elimination of uncertainty. They want decisions to feel definitive in the way that they felt definitive before — before the replays, before the ball-tracking, before the Snicko trace that shows the faintest of edges on a ball given not out. The problem is that the certainty they are nostalgic for was always illusory. Bad decisions were made before DRS. They were simply made invisibly, accepted as final, and moved on from. The DRS made those decisions visible and gave them a remedy. The discomfort this produces — the feeling that cricket is more uncertain than it used to be, that technology has introduced ambiguity rather than resolution — is the discomfort of a game confronting its own fallibility honestly rather than covering it with the fiction of finality. That is not a step away from cricket's humanity. It is a step into it. The game has always been played by fallible people, making fallible decisions, under pressure, in conditions that do not permit perfection. The DRS has not changed that. It has simply made us watch more carefully. And watching more carefully, in cricket as in most things, is rarely the wrong response.
