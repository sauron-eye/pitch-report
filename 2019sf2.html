Sixteen Deliveries That
Ended an Empire: England
Dismantle Australia by 8 Wickets

In the space of sixteen consecutive deliveries — ten from Woakes, six from Archer — Australia lost their two totemic openers, Aaron Finch for a golden duck and David Warner for nine, and the match assumed the character it would hold for the remaining thirty-one overs of play: one side pressing forward, the other searching, with increasing desperation, for a foothold that was never going to come. England were in the World Cup final for the first time since 1992. Australia had lost a semi-final for the first time in their history.

There is a peculiar quality to the silence that falls at Edgbaston — that particular, intimate, old-ground hush — in the moment between a delivery leaving the bowler's hand and whatever comes next. It is not the silence of other grounds. It is the silence of a place that has witnessed too much of England's cricket history to be surprised by any of it, but that retains, nonetheless, the capacity to hold its breath. When Jofra Archer's second delivery of the match — a full-pitched inswinger from around the wicket, directed at the pads of Aaron Finch, who was struck on the flap of the front pad well in line with middle stump — struck its target and the umpire's finger rose, Edgbaston did not observe a silence. It produced a roar that was months of anticipation and twenty-seven years of World Cup semi-final absence given sudden, enormous voice. Finch was gone for a golden duck. The review had confirmed the trajectory in the time it takes to draw a breath. And the match, which had still barely begun, was already resolving itself into the shape it would maintain for the remainder of an extraordinary July afternoon: England, always ahead; Australia, never quite able to close the distance.

The context in which this match took place was not incidental to its drama. Australia had beaten England by sixty-four runs at Lord's a fortnight earlier, a comprehensive defeat that had left England's tournament — and, in the minds of those who track such things, their four years of careful construction under Eoin Morgan — in genuine jeopardy. Australia had won their previous four World Cup matches against England consecutively. They arrived at Edgbaston having lost seven semi-finals in World Cup history and progressed from all seven. They were, by most measures, the tournament's most experienced knockout side, and they were playing against a team who had not stood at this stage of a World Cup since 1992 and who, at Lord's, had looked like a team that could still stumble under the particular weight of expectation that a home World Cup generates. What England then produced at Edgbaston — first with the ball, with a precision and collective intelligence that their Lord's performance had given no particular reason to anticipate at such a level, and then with the bat, in a manner that brooked no argument about who the better team was — was the performance that four years of Morgan's reinvention of English ODI cricket had been, in retrospect, pointing toward.

Woakes, Archer and the Sixteen-Ball Dismantling

Australia had won the toss and elected to bat, reading a surface that offered early movement as one that would be better navigated from the start rather than chased under lights. It was a reasonable assessment that became, within six overs, a catastrophe. Archer's delivery to Finch — a back-of-a-length jag-backer that curved in perfectly, the ball cannoning directly into middle stump — was, technically, the first wicket of the day, falling at the first over's second ball. But the match's essential tone had been established a delivery earlier: Warner, pumped with adrenalin in the way that he always is when the stakes are at their largest, had leaned effortlessly into a first-ball loosener from Woakes and driven it through the covers for four — a boundary that recalled, in its certainty and its ease, every morning of Australian batting ascendancy that England have had to endure across the preceding two decades. Then Woakes regrouped. He moved the ball back half a yard, concentrated his mind, built pressure through the powerplay, and in the fifth delivery of his second over drew Warner into a fend at a back-of-a-length delivery that leaped sharply from the seam, took the edge, and carried to Jonny Bairstow at second slip, diving forward to take a sharp chance in front of his face. Ten for two.

Peter Handscomb, batting at three in the absence of the dropped Usman Khawaja, was nervous from his first ball — shuffling across his crease, inviting the nip-backer, caught in the no-man's-land between defense and attack that describes a batsman who has not decided, on this particular morning, who he intends to be. Woakes reviewed the first lbw shout and it was narrowly umpire's call. The reprieve lasted precisely eleven deliveries. Woakes then bowled him through the inside edge with a full delivery that angled back into the stumps, the gap between bat and pad wide enough for the ball to exploit without difficulty, and Australia were fourteen for three inside six overs. The score seemed to belong to a nightmare, except that it was in every sense the product of rational and systematic bowling by two men — Woakes operating with the seam, Archer operating with pace and the ability to generate disconcerting lift from a good-length area — who had identified the conditions' assistance and applied it with the efficiency of bowlers who know exactly what they are doing and why.

Smith, Carey and the Necessary Resistance

What Steve Smith then produced — eighty-five from a hundred and nineteen deliveries, a fourth consecutive half-century in World Cup knockout matches, the innings of a batsman who has internalized the idea that his particular responsibility in a team of this kind is to provide the mountain when the valley has already appeared — was as fine an effort in defeat as this tournament produced. He found in Alex Carey an ally of unexpected substance. Carey arrived at the crease with the match already severely tilted, batted through a savage bouncer from Archer that opened a gash on his chin and left him bleeding profusely and requiring extensive treatment at the crease, and then — his entire jaw swaddled in bandaging — proceeded to make forty-six from sixty-seven deliveries with the composure of a man who has chosen, consciously, to ignore the pain and focus on the mathematics. Their partnership of a hundred and three from a hundred and twenty-seven balls — the fourth-wicket stand that took Australia from fourteen for three to a hundred and seventeen for four — gave Australia something to build on and gave their supporters a period in which the outcome felt, if not recoverable, at least not yet entirely decided.

Adil Rashid ended it. He had been primed by Morgan for a particular over — the over that the captain had identified, across a tournament of careful planning and patient construction, as the moment his leg-spinner could turn the match from a contest into a coronation. Carey's dismissal was partly self-inflicted: a wander down the pitch, a chip to James Vince stationed on the deep midwicket fence, a shot that sacrificed the wicket without the six it sought. But Rashid's destruction of Marcus Stoinis — a Stoinis who lasted two balls before a ripping googly found his outside edge, beat the outside edge, curved away from a bat angled down the wrong line, and struck the off stump — was the bowling of a man operating at the precise limit of his considerable gifts. Rashid's three-for from ten overs was, in its collective contribution to the containment and dismissal of a middle order that needed to build on the Smith-Carey platform, the performance without which the final margin might have been less comfortable. Glenn Maxwell's dismissal — the knuckleball from Archer, miles slower than the previous delivery and disguised without a perceptible change of action, lobbed to Morgan at short cover as Maxwell drove at the ghost of a delivery that had already decelerated — was the moment at which Australia's resistance stopped being resistance and started being arithmetic.

Smith himself was run out by Buttler for eighty-five — the extraordinary direct hit from a shy at the stumps that wriggled through a narrow gap in Smith's own groin and beat him to the crease — a dismissal that, in its particular category of misfortune, was the kind that retrospect renders nearly inevitable: the batsman who has carried the innings to the edge of something, deprived at the last by the alertness of the wicketkeeper and the vagary of a ball's path through the fielder's most anatomically inconvenient point. Mitchell Starc made twenty-nine from thirty-six in the lower order — the slog that pushed Australia past two hundred — but the final total of two hundred and twenty-three from forty-nine overs was, on the Edgbaston surface with the dew beginning to settle and the conditions flattening for the innings that followed, approximately sixty runs short of what the match required.

"Completely outplayed today. The way they set the tone in those first six overs — there was no way back from that. England were outstanding. Everything they did, they did better than us."

Aaron Finch, Australia captain, post-match press conference at Edgbaston

Roy, Bairstow and the Chase That Made History

England needed two hundred and twenty-four. The number was never, given what had just occurred in forty-nine overs of Australian batting, going to feel like a mountain. But mountains have a way of asserting themselves when the occasion is large enough, and Edgbaston in a World Cup semi-final, with England needing a win they had not managed in a tournament of this magnitude in twenty-seven years, is an occasion whose weight is not entirely reducible to arithmetic. Mitchell Starc had, at Lord's a fortnight before this, taken four for forty-three and had been, in that match, the reason England's chase had collapsed. He was, on this morning too, generating genuine pace and late swing, and the first ten overs of England's chase required from Roy and Bairstow something more than simple confidence. They required a considered management of a bowler who was, in conditions that aided him, among the most dangerous in the world.

What they gave it was bravery combined with intelligence. Roy made fifty from fifty balls — seven fours and two sixes in a fifty that contained one moment of specific grandeur: Nathan Lyon, drafted on in the eleventh over in a search for some form of containment, was hit for six first-ball, a premeditated, entirely contemptuous assault that sent the ball soaring over long-on and told the watching ground, more eloquently than any press conference, what Roy thought of the equation. When Australia, in a further act of desperation, turned to Steve Smith's occasional off-breaks in an attempt to manufacture a breakthrough, Roy struck three consecutive sixes — the third of them landing in the fourth tier of the new Edgbaston stand with the most percussive sound available to a cricket ball meeting seating — and by the end of the seventeenth over the partnership stood at a hundred and twenty-four, Bairstow on thirty-four and Roy on sixty-one, and the match's conclusion had become merely a matter of time and degree. Bairstow fell lbw to Starc for thirty-four, the inswinger finally finding the line it had been searching for; in doing so he used England's review, a decision that would have consequences.

Roy reached eighty-five before the afternoon produced its moment of controversy. Pat Cummins delivered a bouncer that climbed sharply, Roy attempted a pull, and umpire Kumar Dharmasena — who had first appeared to signal a wide before, seemingly swayed by the vehemence of Australia's appeal, reversed the decision and raised his finger — sent him on his way for a dismissal that the replay suggested had involved no contact between ball and bat. Roy, realising that Bairstow's earlier review had been exhausted, was left without recourse. He departed in a fury that was, given the replays, entirely understandable, throwing his gloves across the dressing room in a gesture that Edgbaston's full-length glass facade made visible to the remaining crowd. He was subsequently fined thirty percent of his match fee and collected two demerit points. And England, at a hundred and forty-seven for two with seventy-seven still needed from more than eighteen overs, had Root and Morgan and the certainty that the remaining target was a formality.

Joe Root made forty-nine not out from forty-six deliveries with the assurance of a batsman for whom the chase of a modest target is as natural as breathing, finding the gaps, rotating the strike, never appearing to be in discomfort against the remaining Australian bowling. Morgan made forty-five not out from thirty-nine, including the winning stroke — a heave over long-on off Behrendorff that hit the advertising boards beyond the boundary with the casual authority of a man who has been waiting for the moment and has found it, here, in the thirty-second over at Edgbaston, in his team's first World Cup semi-final in twenty-seven years. The fireworks that the ground had arranged for the occasion went off in the thick July air. The Edgbaston crowd gave England what Edgbaston crowds have, across this tournament, been reliably giving England: the sense of a fortress held, of a fortress possibly never to be surrendered.

What the Scoreline Conceals — and What It Reveals

An eight-wicket victory with a hundred and seven balls remaining, in a World Cup semi-final against Australia, requires certain qualifications if it is to be understood correctly. The first is that Australia were the better team at Lord's a fortnight earlier and that the margin of victory there — sixty-four runs, Behrendorff's five wickets a performance of genuine quality that England's top order had no answer to — was not a deception. The second is that Edgbaston is not Lord's: it is a ground whose conditions, in July, assist the English seam bowler in particular ways that do not translate readily to other venues, and that Woakes — born in Coventry, playing home matches at this ground across his entire career — operates here with a quality of menace that elevates him above the bowler he is elsewhere. The third is that Australia, winning the toss on a surface that clearly offered early movement, made the correct decision and then had three wickets taken in fourteen for three inside six overs before the decision had delivered any of its intended benefits. On another surface, or with another set of Australian batsmen slightly better equipped for the conditions, the morning might have proceeded differently.

All of which is to say that the margin concealed a match that was not, for its first forty overs, entirely a foregone conclusion, and that Smith's eighty-five and Carey's forty-six and the partnership they built between them was genuine resistance rather than mere accumulation. What the margin reveals, however, is something accurate about the relative quality of the two teams on this particular day: England's bowling was better, their fielding was better, and their batting was, when it mattered, sufficiently better to render the final result a comfortable truth. Australia had never lost a World Cup semi-final. The record, like all records, was always going to end. That it ended at Edgbaston — the ground that, across this tournament, had served as England's impenetrable redoubt — felt, in retrospect, less like an upset than a settling of accounts.

There is a famous phrase associated with Edgbaston and its role in English cricket's most charged moments — a sense that the ground does something to visiting sides that other grounds do not, that its enclosed intimacy and its particular crowd produce a pressure of the kind that the game's most consequential matches demand. What England produced here was not merely a product of their home advantage. It was the product of four years of deliberate reconstruction, of a captain who had looked at the rubble of England's 2015 World Cup campaign — eliminated in the group stage, humiliated in their own country — and decided, with the kind of clear-eyed ambition that is rarer in cricket administration than it should be, that the game could be played differently. The batting had been rebuilt around aggression and tempo. The bowling had been diversified to include the raw pace of Archer alongside the craft of Woakes and Rashid. The fielding had been transformed. And here, at the moment the transformation was called upon to produce its ultimate justification, it had done exactly that — and done it, as the greatest performances tend to, before most observers had fully understood that the thing had already been decided. Australia had been the kings of knockout cricket. England had deposed them. Lord's awaited, and with it the final that English cricket had been working, sometimes knowingly and sometimes not, toward since the winter of 2015 when the rubble was first cleared away.
