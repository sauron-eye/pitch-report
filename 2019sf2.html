Roy's Blaze and the Woakes
Dismantling: England March
Into a Final Twelve Years Awaited

At Edgbaston — the ground that has, across this summer, offered England something closer to a fortress than a venue — they did not merely beat Australia. They dismantled them. Woakes removed three in the first ten overs. Roy struck eighty-five from sixty-five. And the nation that invented this game found itself, after twelve years of semi-final absence, back in a World Cup final.

There is a particular kind of cricket match — the kind that England, for most of this tournament, had been threatening to produce without quite delivering — where the margin of victory conceals, rather than reveals, the nature of the contest. England's eight-wicket demolition of Australia at Edgbaston on the eleventh of July was not, despite the scoreline, a comfortable afternoon. It was earned, in the opening ten overs, through bowling of such precision and movement that Australia's much-vaunted batting order found itself three wickets down for fourteen runs inside seven overs, staring at a scoreboard that had no route back to the kind of total that was going to threaten a team that had been, across this World Cup, the most formidable batting unit in the tournament. Finch was gone for a duck — lbw to Archer's first-ball full inswinger, a delivery of such quality that the review lasted less than three seconds — before Warner had been bounced out by Woakes for one, before Handscomb had been bowled through the gate in the sixth over by a nip-backer so perfectly constructed that the Edgbaston crowd greeted it with the kind of extended roar that accumulates when expectation meets delivery. Australia were fourteen for three. The match, though no one was prepared to say so aloud, had already assumed its essential shape.

Australia's recovery — and it was, given the circumstances, a recovery of considerable substance — was engineered almost entirely by Steven Smith. What Smith produced at Edgbaston was, in its way, the finest innings of his tournament: eighty-five from a hundred and nineteen deliveries, the fourth-wicket partnership with Alex Carey worth a hundred and three runs from a hundred and twenty-seven balls, the architecture of an innings that was never going to succeed but that refused to accept the terms of its failure without something approaching a counter-argument. Smith drove through the covers with the wristy elegance that is among the most aesthetically satisfying sights in modern batsmanship. He cut, worked, accumulated. Carey, at the other end, made forty-six with the kind of tidy, unfussy batting that is the mark of a number six who understands that his function is support rather than dominance. Between them they took Australia from fourteen for three to a hundred and seventeen for three — a partnership that gave Australian supporters something to watch and gave the Australian dressing room something to build on, before Carey picked out deep midwicket off Rashid to end the partnership at a moment when England, building pressure through the spinners, had reasserted their command.

Archer, Wood and Rashid: The Anatomy of a Bowling Performance

What distinguished England's bowling from the merely accurate was the variety of its methods and the consistency of its discipline across four spells from four different bowlers, each of whom seemed to understand his particular assignment in the architecture of the innings. Jofra Archer, operating at his highest pace in this tournament — touching ninety-two miles per hour at his fastest — was the shock merchant, the man whose responsibility was to establish, in the first over, that this afternoon was going to be played on England's terms. He did so with a delivery to Finch that was irreversible in its quality: full, swinging, plumb. Chris Woakes — at Edgbaston, always at Edgbaston — was the movement bowler, the man whose natural conditions are this ground's early morning swing, the ball behaving as though it has been arranged to do so. His three for twenty from eight overs was the performance of a bowler who, at his home ground, belongs to a category of menace that does not fully translate to other venues. Adil Rashid, with three wickets from ten overs, was the pressure in the middle, the man whose loop and drift on a pitch that was taking turn by the middle overs gave Australia's remaining batsmen a problem they could not solve through aggression. And Mark Wood, with his particular brand of thunderous straight pace, took the final wicket of Behrendorff with a yorker of searing speed to close Australia's innings at two hundred and twenty-three. Not enough. The Edgbaston surface had confirmed, by the end of the forty-ninth over, what the first-over dismissal of Finch had suggested: this was not a pitch on which to be three down inside seven.

"Totally outplayed today. The way they had us 14 for 3 after six overs set the tone. You expect the new ball to come on any surface — they bowled a great length and that was the match decided."

Aaron Finch, Australia captain, post-match at Edgbaston

Roy and Bairstow: A Chase That Brooked No Argument

England needed two hundred and twenty-four. The mathematics were irrelevant to what Jason Roy then produced, which was something closer to an argument with the nature of run-chasing itself: a refusal to acknowledge that a target, however modest, requires caution. Roy struck eighty-five from sixty-five deliveries — seven fours and two sixes in an innings that, from its first moments, was played at a tempo that Australia's bowlers had not prepared for and could not adjust to quickly enough. The opening partnership, Roy and Bairstow — Bairstow making thirty-four, his dismissal to a Starc inswinger unfortunate in retrospect but unreviewable in the moment — was worth a hundred and twenty-four from seventeen overs. A hundred and twenty-four. In a chase of two hundred and twenty-four. From the first seventeen overs. The match was, at that point, a formality wearing the costume of a contest.

Roy's own dismissal — caught behind off a short ball from Cummins, referred upstairs, hotspot suggesting the thinnest possible contact though Roy himself departed with the controlled fury of a man who does not accept the technology's verdict — ended an innings that was among the finest of this entire World Cup. He threw his gloves in the changing room, by all accounts. He had earned the right. England, at one hundred and forty-seven for two with Joe Root on thirty-three and Eoin Morgan on twenty-four and seventeen overs of batting remaining, were a team that needed only to not embarrass themselves, and they did not. Root drove cleanly, worked crisply, reached forty-nine not out. Morgan struck the winning boundary off Behrendorff with a casual contempt that said, more eloquently than any post-match press conference, what England thought of the afternoon's work. The crowd — Edgbaston at its most fervent, flags and scarves and the particular noise of a partisan English summer crowd that has waited twelve years for a moment exactly like this — gave them what they deserved. England were in the World Cup final. Australia were going home. The Ashes was, for another summer, not yet decided. But the World Cup — this particular, improbable, enormous World Cup — belonged now to England's dream.

Australia: A Campaign That Deserved Better

Australia had arrived at Edgbaston as the tournament's most consistent side: five wins in seven group matches, a net run rate that bore the stamp of comprehensive victories, a bowling attack led by Starc and Cummins that had, on more than one occasion, taken matches away from opponents who had believed themselves to be competitive. They had beaten England at Lord's — Jason Behrendorff's five wickets there still among this World Cup's more remarkable individual performances — and had done so with the kind of authority that suggested the semi-final rematch would be more balanced than the final scoreline indicated. What they could not have known, and could not have prepared for, was the form in which England arrived at Edgbaston: clarified by the experience of a Lord's defeat, elevated by the knowledge that this was, by some distance, the most important match they had played in twelve years, and operating on a ground where their bowlers — Woakes in particular — have, for years, behaved as though the conditions have been prepared specifically for them. The better team won, and the margin concealed very little about the nature of the afternoon. Australia departed with their dignity and their record, and with the knowledge that they were, for the second successive World Cup final, not in it. The next edition beckons.

There is something worth noting, in the aftermath of an afternoon as decisive as this, about what it means for English cricket. Since 1992 — since Imran Khan's charismatic Pakistan defeated them in the final at Melbourne — England have produced batting units, bowling attacks, the occasional transcendent individual, and the structural chaos of repeated semi-final exits. What they had not produced, until Eoin Morgan arranged the terms of its production, was the collective psychological certainty that a team requires to win a tournament rather than merely to compete in one. Whether that certainty survives into the final — and the final brings with it an entirely different order of occasion, New Zealand waiting with Kane Williamson's quiet composure and the bowling of Boult and Henry, weapons that have already, in this tournament, proved unplayable — will be decided in five days' time. For now, though, England had done what they had not done in twenty-seven years. They had won a semi-final. The rest was to come.
