ICC Cricket World Cup 2019 · Match 20 · The Oval, London · 15 June 2019

Finch's 153 and Starc's
Four-For: Australia Move to
the Top of the Table

Aaron Finch came into this match having been dismissed for nought in the previous fixture — a group stage reverse that had, at the time, felt like a warning about the fragility of the best-laid plans in this unpredictable tournament. At The Oval on a crisp June Saturday, he responded with a hundred and fifty-three from a hundred and thirty-two deliveries, the innings of a man who has processed the failure entirely and arrived at the next occasion unencumbered. Australia made three hundred and thirty-four. Mitchell Starc — the tournament's leading wicket-taker already at his seventeenth of the campaign — took four for fifty-five. Sri Lanka, who had produced their own unexpected century from Karunaratne, were dismissed for two hundred and forty-seven. Australia went to the top of the table. The momentum that would carry them to the semi-final was, on this Saturday, firmly established.

The Oval in June has a particular quality of light — a south London brightness that is sharper than the grounds further north, more insistent, less filtered by the industrial ambiguity of Manchester or the woollen softness of Leeds. It is a ground that rewards attacking batting, that has seen more totals above three hundred in the last decade than almost any other venue in England, and that offers the fast bowler in the first hour enough surface to make the defensive option genuinely dangerous. Aaron Finch, arriving at The Oval with the specific purposefulness of a captain who has had two days to consider the golden duck at Trent Bridge against the West Indies and who has decided, with the pragmatism that characterises his captaincy, that purposefulness is the only appropriate response, batted from the first over with the attacking intent that had been notable by its absence in the previous match. He drove Nuwan Pradeep through the covers from the second delivery of the innings with the high elbow and complete weight transfer of a batsman who has arrived at the crease already in motion. He never stopped.

David Warner made his fifty in thirty-eight deliveries — the first fifty of a World Cup innings in which he had been producing significant scores with the regularity of a man who has been given twelve months' enforced rest and has arrived at the tournament with the accumulated appetite of it. He and Finch added eighty-six for the first wicket in the kind of powerplay partnership that sets Australia's total on its trajectory — not through individual excellence alone but through the combined pressure of two openers who trust each other's judgment about when to rotate, when to attack and when to wait for the ball they want. When Warner was dismissed for fifty-six — edged behind off Kasun Rajitha in the fifteenth over, a delivery that moved slightly off the seam and found the gap between bat and pad before Warner had completed the footwork he had been attempting — the partnership had given the innings its shape and Finch had given it its captain.

Finch and Smith: A Partnership of Quality

Steve Smith made seventy-three from fifty-nine deliveries in the kind of innings that, in another match on another day, would have been the conversation's centrepiece. His pull shot off Lasith Malinga — a pull shot hit with extraordinary timing and placed through the midwicket gap with the flat-bat precision that distinguishes the highest quality of this stroke from its more agricultural relatives — was the shot that best illustrated the quality of batting that Australia were producing. He cut Dhananjaya de Silva through point with the crisp dismissiveness of a batsman who has identified the line and committed to the shot entirely. And he was involved in a second-wicket stand with Finch of one hundred and twenty-seven that, across its twenty-two overs, moved Australia from eighty-six for one to a position where three hundred and fifty felt entirely attainable. Smith's dismissal — caught at mid-on by Kusal Perera off a de Silva delivery that was fuller than he had expected — removed the partnership but could not slow the innings. Finch continued.

The hundred came in the twenty-ninth over from a hundred and two deliveries — Finch acknowledging it with the restrained celebration of a batsman who regards the century not as an achievement complete in itself but as the platform from which something larger is to be constructed. He struck his fourth and fifth sixes in the thirty-sixth and thirty-eighth overs, long-on clearances of considerable power that established the final character of the innings as one of aggression rather than merely accumulation. Glenn Maxwell's forty-six from twenty-five deliveries — three sixes and two fours in a cameo that took Australia from two hundred and sixty-eight for five to a final total of three hundred and thirty-four — was the distilled essence of what Maxwell contributes to this batting order: an ability to accelerate the innings's rate in its final stages to a degree that makes a total competitive regardless of what has been scored in the first forty-five overs. Finch was dismissed in the forty-eighth over for one hundred and fifty-three — the last of his thirteen boundaries an edge to deep point, a dismissal with the particular quality of imprecision that signals a batsman who has extracted his maximum from the innings and no longer has the reserves to be entirely precise.

"It was a pretty good day for us. When you lose a toss at a good batting ground, you want to come out and make a statement. I'm really happy with how we batted."

Aaron Finch, Australia captain and Player of the Match, post-match at The Oval

Karunaratne's Audacity and Starc's Reckoning

Sri Lanka's reply began with the specific boldness that their captain had decided, in the absence of a tactical alternative, was the only available approach: attack the Australian opening bowling from the first over and establish a run rate that would make the target reachable before the conditions deteriorated and the pace bowlers found their rhythm. Dimuth Karunaratne and Kusal Perera opened together and put on one hundred and fifteen in the first fifteen overs — a partnership that had, in its early stages, the character of an upset in construction, the kinds of boundaries accumulating at a rate that made the Australian fielding positions look reactive rather than proactive. Perera's fifty-two was made with the muscular, wristy aggression of a left-hander who has identified the cut and the pull as his primary weapons and who was finding, on The Oval's quick outfield, that both were producing the boundaries he required. Karunaratne batted with greater control, driving cleanly through the covers and working the spinners through midwicket, and reached ninety-seven before a Jason Behrendorff delivery found his outside edge — a delivery of full, late swing that confirmed, for Karunaratne, that the innings he had played was exceptional and that the three runs required for a century had remained, to his visible frustration, three runs too far.

The middle-order collapse was the predictable conclusion to a partnership that had begun to exhaust its momentum. Starc's four wickets — Kusal Mendis, Angelo Mathews, Thisara Perera and Lasith Malinga — were taken with the full-pitched, high-pace deliveries that are his most devastating weapon on surfaces that offer him bounce and carry, the leg before wicket and the caught behind arriving with the regularity of outcomes that had been inevitable from the moment the partnership broke. Kusal Mendis was beaten for pace by a Starc delivery that straightened off the seam and found the stumps. Mathews gloved a short ball down the leg side to Carey — a dismissal that ended the one Sri Lanka batsman who might have mounted a recovery. Kane Richardson took three for forty-seven to complete the bowling rout, his movement off the seam providing the variety that made the Starc-Richardson combination, across this innings, as effective as any bowling pairing in the tournament. Sri Lanka were bowled out for two hundred and forty-seven. Australia moved to the top of the group stage table with four wins from five.

The eighty-seven-run margin understated Australia's superiority across the match's full hundred overs. Sri Lanka's one hundred and fifteen opening stand had been the innings's one exceptional passage — and had Karunaratne converted his ninety-seven, had the middle order found the resolution its composition suggested, the match might have been different. But cricket at this level rarely rewards what might have been, and the middle-order collapse — four wickets in thirty-three deliveries — demonstrated that Sri Lanka's batting beyond their top two lacked the depth to sustain a chase of three hundred and thirty-five against Starc and Richardson in conditions that rewarded their skills. For Australia, Finch's hundred and fifty-three was the landmark, but the substance of their advance to the top of the table lay in the accumulation of evidence — across five matches now — that they had the batting depth to post large totals and the bowling quality to defend them.
