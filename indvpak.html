Seven-Nil: Rohit, Kohli and the Unrelenting Mathematics of an India-Pakistan World Cup

One billion television viewers. Seven hundred thousand ticket requests for fifty thousand seats. The most anticipated match of the tournament since the day the fixtures were published. Cricket's great geopolitical rivalry — a contest that carries more history, more emotion, more weight than any other fixture in the game's calendar — arrived at Old Trafford on a grey Manchester Sunday, took a deep breath, and produced what India-Pakistan World Cup matches almost always produce: an Indian victory of comfortable, occasionally dominant proportions. Seven-nil. On the biggest stage, the rivalry's arithmetic remains stubbornly, irresistibly one-sided.

There is a particular kind of anticipation that builds around India-Pakistan matches that has no equivalent anywhere else in sport. It is not merely the anticipation of competitive sport, though there is that. It is the anticipation of a cultural event — a moment when two nations that share history, language, music, food, and a border defined by one of the most consequential partitions in the twentieth century, express the full complexity of that relationship through the medium of a cricket match. Old Trafford on the sixteenth of June had been preparing for this expression since the World Cup draw was made. The ground was sold out. The atmosphere was the loudest the tournament had produced. The match, having consumed all this expectation, proceeded to be almost entirely one-sided.

Pakistan won the toss and put India in under overcast skies that suggested swing and seam movement would be available to their bowlers in the first ten overs. It was, objectively, the correct decision. Mohammad Amir, bowling from the Pavilion End with the long, raking action that makes his left-arm over-the-wicket trajectory so distinctive, found swing in his opening overs and would, across his ten overs, take three wickets for 47 runs — his performance the one genuinely impressive Pakistani bowling display in a day of otherwise imbalanced cricket. But Amir, for all his quality, was bowling to Rohit Sharma and KL Rahul, and these were not batsmen who would be defeated by swing bowling before they had settled.

Rohit and Rahul: The 136-Run Opening Stand That Set the Tone

Rohit Sharma came to this match on the back of his 122 not out against South Africa and with the particular focused calm of a batsman who has found form and intends to maintain it. He and Rahul negotiated the powerplay with the organised patience of a partnership that has decided to take nothing from the bowling attack — neither easy runs nor careless wickets — and to convert the early restraint into an accelerating middle-overs platform. The fifty partnership arrived from 78 balls, the hundred from 124, and by the time the thirteenth over arrived, India had 53 runs on the board and Pakistan's fielding attack — three dropped chances in the first fifteen overs, each one costing runs that compounded the opening partnership's accumulation — had conceded, through a combination of poor throwing and misreads of the drive, the boundaries that the bowling had been trying to prevent.

The most egregious of Pakistan's fielding errors came in the eleventh over when Fakhar Zaman, stationed at deep midwicket, received a throw from backward point with Rohit halfway down the pitch at the other end. He threw to the wrong end — the bowler's end, where Rahul was safely in — and Rohit, who had been three metres short of his ground when the throw was received, walked home without incident. It was, the crowd recognised immediately, an error that would cost Pakistan runs: Rohit eventually made 140 from 113 balls, of which seventy-something runs were scored after the dropped chance. The mathematics of Pakistan's fielding was not, on this day, in their favour.

Rahul was dismissed by Wahab Riaz for 57 — caught at cover attempting a drive that was perhaps half-a-yard too full to drive with full comfort — and their stand of 136 was the innings's foundation. Kohli arrived and batted with the particular urgency of a batsman who has watched the powerplay from the pavilion and arrives already calibrated to the pitch's pace. His 77 from 65 balls included nine boundaries — the cover drive, as always with Kohli, played with the full extension and the high elbow that generates pace rather than force — and his partnership with Rohit added 98 for the second wicket, the second successive substantial stand that ensured whatever followed in the India innings would be added to a total already deep in the 200s.

Rohit's 140: The Most Complete India-Pakistan World Cup Innings

Rohit Sharma's 140 from 113 balls, his second century of the tournament in three matches and the highest score made in any India-Pakistan World Cup contest, was an innings of such fluent, controlled brilliance that it made the occasion's pressure — the noise, the billion viewers, the specific weight of cricket's most important rivalry — seem to exist in a separate dimension from the batting itself. He pulled Amir in the twenty-second over with the precise, decisive timing of a man who has been playing this shot since childhood and has refined it, through twenty years of professional cricket, to the point where the mechanics are so deeply embedded that they require no conscious instruction. He drove Hassan Ali through extra cover in the twenty-seventh over and the ball reached the boundary before any fielder had initiated a movement. He swept Shadab Khan, the leg-spinner who had opened the bowling and who had claimed Roy lbw in the England match, with the top hand controlling the shot's direction and the bottom hand providing the acceleration — a stroke that left the Old Trafford pitch and arrived at the midwicket boundary in what felt, to those watching, like an unreasonably short space of time.

He fell for 140 in the thirty-eighth over — a slightly cheeky attempt to scoop Hassan Ali over fine leg that found the fielder's hands after Rohit had shuffled across his stumps with the intention of wrong-footing the field. The dismissal was, in character, entirely consistent with Rohit at his most liberated: a batsman who has scored enough runs to feel that he is entitled to attempt the inventive shot, and who occasionally miscalculates the execution of the entitlement. By that point, India were 244 for 2 in the thirty-eighth over and the total they were building had the look of something that Pakistan, in the conditions that would follow, could not realistically pursue.

Kohli and Pandya extended the platform: Kohli to 77 before Amir's swinging delivery found the outside edge, Pandya to 26 from 19 balls with the uninhibited striking of a lower-middle-order batsman who has been given clear instructions about his role and has interpreted them liberally. India ended their innings at 336 for 5. The total, under grey Manchester skies on a pitch that had flattened but not lost all its early variation, was formidable. Whether formidable enough depended on Pakistan's batting, which had been, for all of their best performances in this tournament, inconsistent against quality bowling.

Pakistan's Chase: The Collapse That Ended All Hope

Pakistan began their chase of 337 in the manner of a side that had been here before — specifically at Trent Bridge two weeks earlier, where a chase of 349 had ended 14 runs short and where their batting order had at least produced two centuries in a losing cause. Here, against India's bowling, they found themselves in a different and more profoundly inadequate position from the first over. Imam-ul-Haq was dismissed for 7 in the fifth over by Bhuvneshwar Kumar, who found the outswinger's angle with the kind of precision that suggests a bowler has spent his training sessions working specifically on the shape of delivery required to beat this specific batsman's defensive technique. At 13 for one, Pakistan needed their most technically accomplished batsmen — Babar Azam and Fakhar Zaman — to build the partnership that would give the chase its foundation.

Fakhar and Babar built 104 together — a stand of considerable quality, marked by Fakhar's characteristic front-foot aggression and Babar's equally characteristic off-side precision. Fakhar made 62 from 75 balls, including two boundaries off Bumrah that the crowd received with the particular noise made when an opposition batsman hits the best bowler in the world for four: grudging, but genuinely impressed. Babar made 48 from 57 with his customary cover drive dominating the shot selection. For a period in the middle overs, from approximately the fourteenth to the twenty-third, Pakistan were scoring at seven and eight an over and the target — still over 200, still in need of an acceleration Pakistan had not yet produced consistently in this tournament — was distant but not impossibly so.

And then Kuldeep Yadav arrived. The left-arm wrist spinner, playing his first competitive match in several weeks after being rested, bowled Babar with a delivery that the batsman had not fully read — a wrong-un that gripped and turned at the point of impact to clip the top of off stump in a manner that left Babar standing at the crease for a moment, looking at the pitch, with the expression of a batsman who has been bowled by something he had categorised incorrectly. Fakhar, attempting a sweep in Kuldeep's following over, top-edged the ball to fine leg and departed for 62. Pakistan were 126 for 3. Then, in the following over from Pandya, Hafeez was bowled — trapped against the stumps by a slower delivery that came back in — and Malik, facing the very next ball, was caught at short mid-wicket. Two wickets in two balls from Pandya: the bat-pad combination that had made him, in the tournament's earlier weeks, an unexpectedly dangerous middle-overs bowler. Pakistan were 129 for 5 in the twenty-seventh over, needing 208 from 23 overs with five wickets down, and the match was effectively over.

Rain arrived at 18:20 BST, interrupting the chase that was already lost, and when play resumed 55 minutes later, the DLS calculation had revised Pakistan's target upward into the genuinely absurd: 302 in 40 overs, meaning 136 from 30 balls from a position of 6 wickets down. The ground had, in the rain's intermission, gone two-thirds empty — the Pakistani supporters, whose noise had been the match's most exhilarating early feature, having departed in the knowledge that no reasonable calculation placed their team in this game. Sarfaraz Ahmed patted the ball around for the final ten overs in front of an audience that was experiencing the specific discomfort of watching a sporting event whose result has been determined but which must continue for its allotted time. India won by 89 runs on DLS. The record read seven-nil.

The post-match conversation, as it always does after an India-Pakistan World Cup match, ran swiftly to the rivalry's record and its meaning. Seven-nil. Pakistan have not beaten India in a World Cup match since the competition's history began. The question of why this is so has generated analysis, speculation, and occasional mythology in quantities that bear no relationship to the game's actual statistical significance. The explanation most commonly offered — that India's superior resources produce superior players and superior preparation — is correct but insufficient. The full explanation involves, additionally, the specific pressure of the occasion: the way Pakistan's batting, in World Cup cricket against India, tends to produce the brittle middle-overs collapse that defeats them regardless of the solid starts that precede it. Rohit at 140 and Kohli at 77 were the day's foremost performers. But it was Kuldeep Yadav's wrong-un to Babar Azam — the ball that bowled the most technically correct batsman in Pakistan's lineup and immediately triggered the collapse of 4 wickets for 12 runs — that decided the match. It always comes down to that moment: the delivery that breaks the partnership and releases the anxiety that seven World Cup defeats have accumulated in the bones of Pakistan's batting order.

The match ended in the manner in which India-Pakistan World Cup matches tend to end: with one side celebrating and the other side attempting to explain what happened to the batting in the twenty-fifth to the twenty-seventh over. Rohit Sharma, MOM for the second time in three games, said he was pleased with the win and the performance. Sarfaraz Ahmed said his team needed to do better. The scoreboard said seven-nil. Old Trafford, at the end of its long and famous day, said nothing, as all cricket grounds eventually do — preserving within its stones the noise that the crowd made and the batting that produced it, without comment and without judgment.
