Match Report — Day Four, Final Session

What Stokes Did at Headingley

Against all arithmetic, against all probability, against the scorebook's cold verdict of 286 for 9 chasing 359, Benjamin Andrew Stokes walked to the crease at Headingley and refused, simply and categorically, to accept the game that was already written. What followed was not merely an innings. It was an act of will so complete, so absolute in its refusal of defeat, that it asked larger questions of sport than sport normally permits itself to answer.

By Muhammad Hamza Headingley, Leeds 25 August 2019

There is a particular quality to dread in English cricket. It accumulates slowly, over days and years, in the manner of silt deposited by a river that has flooded too many times. On the morning of the twenty-fifth of August 2019, that dread was present in Headingley's stands with its full historical weight intact — the weight of a nation that had sat down to watch England bat and had too often risen to find the innings already over, the target impossible, the cause lost. England needed 203 more runs to win with seven wickets in hand when the fourth and final day began. Joe Root, the captain and the last of the recognized batting, was still there. Ben Stokes, at number six, had spent all of the previous evening in the company of Root, accumulating runs with the careful, almost forensic patience of a man who understood that this was a contest of attrition before it was one of flourish. The scoreboard said possible. The collective memory of everyone present said otherwise.

The context in which this day arrived requires, briefly, its own accounting. Australia had come to Headingley one up in a five-match series, having won at Edgbaston and drawn at Lord's in a match that had ended with Steve Smith, their transcendent batsman, being concussed by a Jofra Archer bouncer and ruled out for this Test. England, who had been demolished for 67 in their first innings — Josh Hazlewood's five-wicket burst triggering one of those spectacular English collapses that feel, in the watching, almost deliberately theatrical — had found themselves set 359 to win, a target that was not merely improbable but, by the long ledger of Test cricket history, almost laughable. No England side had ever chased down anything approaching it in a fourth innings at Headingley. The odds, assembled by the statisticians and corroborated by the common sense of the watching world, suggested that this match was, to all practical purposes, over.

Stokes, of course, had not consulted the statisticians. He had, instead, batted through the third evening with Root in a partnership of accumulating significance, moving from 156 for 3 at the start of that session to 156 for 3 — Root and Stokes had added nothing yet, the day ending with a sense of suspended animation, the innings paused at the edge of one of cricket's great questions. When day four began, Headingley came in from the car parks with the particular tightness of a crowd that expects the worst but has purchased its ticket in some residual hope that the worst might be averted.

The Morning's Slow Unravelling

The day began with Root at 75 not out and Stokes, who had batted the previous evening with a studied, almost monk-like restraint, on 61 from 174 deliveries. The first ten balls of the day Stokes received, he scored from none of them. He was not scratching or nurdling or surviving by the skin of his edges. He was doing something more deliberate and, in its own fashion, more ruthless: he was removing from his own batting the element of risk, compressing his game into its defensible core, accepting the mathematics of attrition rather than the romance of aggression. The ball was doing enough off a surface that retained some morning moisture. Cummins and Hazlewood, the best fast-bowling partnership in the world at that time, were bowling with the disciplined menace that had dismissed England for 67. Every over was a negotiation. Stokes negotiated.

Root fell in the thirteenth over of the day, stumped — stumped, of all methods; a rare and somehow perfect Headingley cruelty — off Nathan Lyon for 77, having danced down the pitch in a moment of misjudgement that seemed out of character and yet was entirely in keeping with the fitful, uneven nature of this England batting performance. Lyon, the off-spinner who would become a malevolent presence throughout the day's drama, took the wicket that lifted him past Dennis Lillee into third on Australia's all-time wicket-taking list. It was a notable landmark, yet in the theatre of the afternoon's events, it would be remembered only as one more cruelty inflicted on the host nation. Root departed for 77, and with him departed the last of England's recognized batting, leaving Stokes alone with the lower order and a target that had contracted to 203 but remained, by any sane reckoning, out of reach.

Jonny Bairstow arrived and played with the combative confidence of a man who has never quite accepted that lower-middle order is where he belongs. He and Stokes put on 86 — a partnership that gave the match its narrative midpoint, a span of batting that kept the scoreboard ticking and the crowd's voice from entirely fading to resignation. Stokes began to open his shoulders, the restraint of the previous eighteen hours slowly, fractionally, giving way to assertion. He clipped Lyon through the leg side. He drove Cummins on the up. The hundred partnership was visible on the horizon, the chance of victory beginning — faintly, tentatively — to reorganize itself from impossible into merely improbable. And then, with England at 238 for 4, Bairstow edged Hazlewood behind for 36, and the slide began.

The Collapse: Joy Deferred, Dread Restored

What happened between Bairstow's dismissal and the arrival of Jack Leach at the crease was one of those passages of Test cricket that feel, as they unfold, like a controlled detonation. Jos Buttler came in and survived nine balls, scoring a single, before a characteristic Stokes calling error — that irrepressible "yes, yes, yes" followed immediately by an equally urgent "no, no, no" — sent him hurtling down the pitch into a direct hit from Travis Head at midwicket. One run scored, run out, and the crowd that had been beginning to stir into hope sagged back into its seats. Chris Woakes, on the back of three consecutive Ashes thirties and a hundred against India, lasted eight balls. He was caught behind off Hazlewood for one, a ball that nibbled off the surface and took the outside edge with terrible economy of effort.

Jofra Archer, at least, swung. He made 15 with the uninhibited purpose of a bowler-batsman who has understood his brief — to be entertaining, to be brief, and to hand the strike back to Stokes as quickly as possible. He was eventually caught at deep square leg off Lyon attempting to clear the boundary, and the mood in the ground shifted once more. England were 261 for 7, needing 98, and the arithmetic was moving in one direction only. When Stuart Broad arrived and lasted two balls before Cummins bowled him for a duck, the entire weight of 98 runs fell upon one man and a number-eleven batsman who, at a different Ashes Test the same summer, had scored 92 against Ireland in a moment of batting comedy that had delighted everyone. On that August 25 afternoon, no one in Headingley was laughing. Stokes stood at 61. England needed 73. Jack Leach walked out wearing spectacles that he would, during the partnership, clean periodically with his shirt tail in a gesture that became, in the hours that followed, one of the summer's enduring images.

One Man, One Job Each: The Last-Wicket Partnership

The roles, once Leach arrived, were established with the wordless clarity of a plan that needed no articulation. Stokes would score. Leach would survive. These were not interchangeable functions and both men understood it. The number eleven's contribution to the 76-run partnership would amount to one run — a single, nudged into the leg side in the final over. Everything else was Stokes. And yet Leach's contribution was not nothing. It was, in its own fashion, the foundation upon which everything else rested. He had to face, across seventeen overs, the most skilled and pressurized bowling attack in the world, including Nathan Lyon spinning the ball viciously on a fourth-day pitch with a tail-ender's end to aim at. The fact that he survived was not fortune entirely. It was the product of technique, nerve, and a refusal to be intimidated that, in retrospect, deserves its own small accounting.

Stokes, from the moment Leach arrived, was a different batsman. The patience that had defined his innings through the previous eighteen hours — the dead bat, the shoulder arms, the disciplined leave — was not abandoned. It was supplemented. He began to read the bowling with the predatory clarity of a batsman who had fully understood what was needed and had decided, with characteristic directness of intention, to do it. Nathan Lyon, who had been threading the ball through bat and pad with dangerous accuracy throughout the day, was suddenly being played off the back foot with contemptuous certainty, then dragged through midwicket, then swept to the boundary, then — in a moment that required not merely skill but audacity — switch-hit for six over deep cover in one of the most extraordinary improvisations the game has produced under pressure. The crowd, which had been oscillating between hope and resignation all day, found a single, locked-in pitch and stayed there.

Cummins, perhaps the best fast bowler in the world, was dispatched back over his head for a six off the first ball of one over — a shot that landed in the stands with the emphatic finality of a statement. Hazlewood was hit for back-to-back sixes in a passage of batting that reduced three deliveries to eighteen runs in a sequence that seemed to belong to a different match, a different competition, a different version of the laws of probability. The scoreboard was no longer ticking. It was accelerating. With each boundary, with each maximum, the crowd's noise moved from encouragement to something closer to disbelief, and then from disbelief to something older and less articulate — the sound a crowd makes when it witnesses something it cannot yet process as real.

There were, in this passage, the full gallery of cricket's minor miracles and catastrophes compressed into a single afternoon's play. Marcus Harris, at third man, put down a sharp but catchable chance when Stokes was on 34 — an early reprieve that could only be appreciated fully in retrospect. A second drop, this time with Stokes on 116, compounded Australia's generosity. The bowling, so disciplined for three days, began to fray at the edges. Misfields accumulated. The fielders, who had been languid with the presumption of victory, were suddenly chasing leather at the boundary. Stokes reached his century — unbeaten, in case the point needs underlining, from the number-six position, in the fourth innings of a Test match England had been set 359 to win — without removing his helmet or raising his bat. There was no time for ceremony. There were still runs to make, and the man who needed to make them understood that completely.

Australia's Undoing: Fortune Favoured the Brave

Cricket is not merely a game of skill. It is also, and the 2019 Headingley Test proved this with remorseless clarity, a game of decisions made under pressure that can, on a bad day, undo everything that skill has built. Australia's undoing arrived in two related moments that will be examined by their players for as long as any of them remain in the game.

The first was Tim Paine's decision to use his last remaining DRS review on a leg-side yorker from Cummins to Jack Leach — a delivery that was patently missing the leg stump and that virtually everyone watching, including Jonathan Agnew at the Test Match Special microphone, identified as a waste before the third umpire's screen had even flickered to life. The review was confirmed as not out within seconds. Australia were review-less. They had, in a single optimistic gesture, surrendered the insurance that every captain protects as though it were currency — which, in the closing overs of a Test match, it precisely is.

The cost was immediate and catastrophic. Several deliveries later, Nathan Lyon — bowling with great accuracy and application, the final over of his magnificent day — found Ben Stokes's pad with a delivery angled in from round the wicket. The ball struck Stokes on the back pad, plumb in front. The appeal went up with collective urgency, and umpire Joel Wilson, after consideration, shook his head. The gathered television analysis of the ball's trajectory suggested that the decision was, at minimum, extremely close — some readings had it as missing, some had it grazing the top of off stump. Australia could not know for certain. They could not review. Tim Paine stood at the wicket and stared at Wilson with the expression of a man who has just understood the full weight of his earlier decision. The game moved on.

The Fumble That History Will Never Forget

And then came Nathan Lyon. With two runs needed, facing the last ball but one of the penultimate over — Lyon's over — Stokes played a reverse sweep to the point fielder. It was a gentle, awkward delivery off the edge rather than the full face of the bat, and in the scramble of the moment Leach, backing up at the non-striker's end, found himself halfway down the pitch. Stokes, instinctively, sent him back. Leach turned. Lyon moved to the ball at point, gathered the return throw, and had the stumps in front of him and Jack Leach stranded many yards short of his ground. He fumbled. The ball popped from his hands, bounced over the stumps, and settled in the outfield. Lyon stood for a moment and looked at his hands with the expression of a man consulting the evidence of a catastrophe he cannot yet believe. The stumps were intact. Leach was safe. He would later say the fumble would haunt him forever, even though they won. Lyon offered a different explanation the following year — a sprinkler head on the pitch that had disrupted his footing — but the image of the ball spilling over the stumps endures regardless of cause, fixed in the memory of everyone who watched it.

Two deliveries later, Lyon bowled the last ball of the over to Stokes, who attempted a sweep and missed, the ball thudding into his back pad in front of the stumps. The roar of the appeal was instantaneous, full-throated, and entirely confident. It looked out. Television replays suggested it looked out. Umpire Joel Wilson shook his head. Australia had no review. Stokes had survived at 131. Justin Langer, watching from the Australian dressing room balcony, hit a dustbin. Cummins stood at mid-on and stared at the sky with the expression of a man taking stock of the ways in which fortune had conspired against him. There were two runs needed. There was one wicket left. And there was Ben Stokes, not out, on strike.

The Final Act: The Cut Shot Heard Round the World

What happened in the following over is a matter of cricketing record, but record alone does not capture what it felt like to witness it. Leach, facing Cummins, was the fifth ball of the over, worked a single to deep square leg — a single greeted in Headingley as though it were a century, the crowd erupting at Leach's survival with an exuberance usually reserved for the fall of wickets in the opposition's innings. Stokes was now on strike with one run needed and Pat Cummins — the best fast bowler in the world — running in. The first ball was defended. The second ball was defended. The third ball — and this, if we are being precise, is the shot — Stokes rocked back and cut Cummins through the covers, the ball racing to the boundary with the particular velocity of a shot struck from the very meat of the bat, and Headingley lost itself entirely. The stands became a single organism of noise and motion. Players streamed from the England dressing room. Stokes raised his arms, bat aloft, and looked skyward, and the man who had spent the previous day and a half in the most disciplined, contained, almost ascetic exercise of concentration in recent English cricket history was, for the first and only time in those two days, entirely unguarded.

Steve Smith, injured and absent from the Test, was waiting at the boundary when Stokes walked off. He said, simply, "unbelievable mate." It was the only adequate response. Australia's captain Tim Paine, generous in the bitterest defeat, called it "one of the great innings." He was not wrong, and the generosity of the assessment was not diminished by the fact that his own decisions — that squandered review, those dropped catching opportunities — had made its consummation possible. Great innings require great circumstances, and sometimes great circumstances require the opposition's collaboration. Stokes had made 135 not out from 219 balls, of which 74 had come in the final partnership with Leach. He had hit eleven fours and eight sixes. He had batted for 219 deliveries across four days. He had bowled twenty-four overs of medium pace in Australia's second innings before coming in to bat. He had done all of this on a body that, by the end of the match, bore the visible marks of everything it had been asked to contribute.

The Man and the Moment: What Stokes Means

In cricket, as in most enduring things, there are two kinds of great performances. The first kind is the expected one: the century from a settled batsman in a position of comfort, the five-wicket haul from a bowler in full command of favourable conditions. These are performances that confirm what we already knew — that talent, expressed under circumstances that permit its expression, will produce extraordinary things. The second kind is rarer and more costly. It is the performance that demands not merely the full deployment of a player's talent, but the full deployment of their character. Stokes at Headingley belonged emphatically to the second category.

What he produced on that Sunday was not, in aggregate, technically perfect batting. He was dropped twice. He was nearly run out. He was beaten by Lyon more times than his eventual innings would suggest. But his batting had a quality that purely technical perfection cannot replicate: the quality of a man who has decided, at some fundamental level below conscious thought, that he will not allow this game to be lost on his watch. That decision — and in some athletes it is recognisably a decision, a commitment made early and honoured regardless of the cost — is what separated this innings from the merely very good and placed it in the company of the genuinely historic. The World Cup final had been six weeks earlier. Stokes had held England's nerves together on that same super over in the same way — not by being flawless, but by being immovable. Two games in six weeks that will be spoken of in the same breath for as long as either sport is played.

There is a philosophical argument, sometimes made about sport, that its dramas are essentially trivial — that compared with the real urgencies of the world, the question of whether an Ashes series is levelled at Headingley on a Sunday afternoon in August is not one that should occupy the adult mind with any great seriousness. This argument contains a truth that is difficult to refute in the abstract. It collapses entirely, however, in the presence of what Ben Stokes did that day. Because what he demonstrated — patience in extremity, courage under sustained pressure, the willingness to absorb pain and complexity and uncertainty and keep going anyway — is not, in the end, a merely cricketing quality. It is a quality that cricket, at its greatest, makes visible and legible in a way that few other human endeavours manage. You saw it in every blocked single, every leave of Lyon, every switch-hit six, every moment he walked back to his mark and prepared, again, to face the next ball. You saw, in essence, what it looks like when a person decides not to give up.

The five-match series would end, at The Oval, in a 2-2 draw. The Ashes were retained by Australia. But to measure this match by the series result is to measure Chartres Cathedral by its floor plan. It is to miss the thing entirely. What happened at Headingley on the twenty-fifth of August 2019 will be watched and re-watched long after the series result has been forgotten, long after the precise statistical details have blurred, long after the players themselves have retired and been replaced by the next generation of men who will stand at the crease with something left to prove. It will be watched because it is, in the most basic and irreducible sense, a great story. And in cricket, as in everything, a great story endures.

When it was done, when the four from Cummins had beaten the fielders and settled against the hoarding, Stokes stood for a moment in the middle of the ground with his bat raised and said nothing. There was nothing left to say. He had made his statement in the only language available to him — a cut shot, a cover drive, eight sixes, a hundred and thirty-five not out — and the twenty thousand people who had spent their Sunday watching him make it were already answering, loudly enough for the whole of a cold Yorkshire evening to hear.
