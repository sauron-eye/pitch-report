ICC Cricket World Cup 2019 · Match 17 · The County Ground, Taunton · 12 June 2019

Warner's Return: A Century
After the Wilderness, and
Amir's Five That Couldn't Save Pakistan

It was, by any measure, the match of two halves that cricket occasionally arranges as if to illustrate a point about the game's resistance to settled conclusions. David Warner, returning from a twelve-month ball-tampering ban with his confidence partially restored and his reputation wholly contested, batted at Taunton like a man settling a debt — scoring his first international hundred since the Cape Town affair, steering Australia to what looked like a total of effortless dominance, and then watching Mohammad Amir dismantle the lower order with five wickets for thirty runs to reduce three-hundred-and-fifty to three hundred and seven. Pakistan, given a target they might have chased, performed the usual trick of looking controlled and then catastrophically losing four wickets for twenty-four runs at the very moment control had felt permanent. Australia won by forty-one runs. The result flattered neither side's middle overs.

Taunton in June is the kind of ground that invites the imagination rather than restraining it — a compact, intimate venue where a county ground's charm has not been entirely surrendered to the ambitions of international cricket, where the boundary is short enough that the ball finds the rope with regularity and the crowd, pressed close to the action, participates in the match rather than merely observing it. It was here, at the County Ground, that David Warner played the innings he needed to play: his first international century since the events of Cape Town fourteen months earlier, during which he had participated in a ball-tampering scheme that had sent him home from South Africa and into an exile that had lasted, in formal terms, exactly one year. The return — to international cricket, to form, and now, in the fourteenth over of this World Cup match, to the three-figure landmark — was completed when an edge flew through the gap between wicketkeeper and wide slip for a boundary that brought up the hundred to considerable noise from the Taunton crowd, some fraction of which was entirely genuine and the remainder of which carried the ambivalence that has followed Warner through every ground in England since his reinstatement to the Australian side.

Pakistan's decision to field first after winning the toss — a call that the damp outfield and overcast sky had seemed to justify at the moment the coin landed — began to look less convincing once Warner and Aaron Finch had negotiated the first ten overs with the patient accumulation of batsmen who have registered the surface's early life and decided, collectively, that patience will be rewarded once the conditions settle. They added one hundred and forty-six in twenty-two overs for the first wicket, the highest partnership of the tournament to that point — a stand that had, within it, the full range of the qualities that make both batsmen, when operating together, among the most intimidating opening combinations in world cricket. Finch drove through the covers with the authority of a captain whose personal form gives the team's approach a confidence that filtering downward. Warner cut, pulled and drove with the crisp aggression that his batting has always carried as its most recognisable signature — the flat-bat pull through midwicket that few batsmen can time with his specific ferocity, the cover drive played close to the body and hit with the full face of the bat rather than its edge.

Amir's Masterclass and the Collapse That Narrowed the Target

Mohammad Amir had taken three wickets in Australia's group stage match against Afghanistan six days earlier. At Taunton, he produced something considerably more significant: five wickets for thirty runs from ten overs, a spell of sustained, intelligent left-arm swing bowling that dismantled Australia's middle and lower order with the efficiency of a craftsman who has spent enough years bowling with a new ball in overcast English conditions to understand precisely what the surface will give him and precisely when it will give it. His figures were, in the immediate context, extraordinary. Warner was dismissed by Amir in the thirty-fifth over for a hundred and seven, caught behind as he attempted to cut a delivery that swung later than he had anticipated — the over after his century, the luxury of celebration abruptly curtailed. And then, between the thirty-seventh and forty-eighth overs, Amir removed Nathan Coulter-Nile, Pat Cummins, Mitchell Starc and Kane Richardson in a spell that transformed what had been shaping as a total of three-hundred-and-fifty or higher into three hundred and seven — a target that remained challenging but had been stripped of the psychological weight that numbers in the three-fifties carry against an attack as capable as Pakistan's.

The last six Australian wickets fell for thirty runs. This statistic requires its own paragraph, not because it is unprecedented in World Cup cricket — collapses of this kind have, over the years, become an occupational hazard of the lower order's ambition — but because it altered the match's character so entirely. Australia's bowlers, walking out to defend three hundred and seven rather than three hundred and fifty, were operating in a different contest from the one the first thirty overs had suggested they would face. The margin between those figures is not merely forty-three runs on a scorecard; it is the difference between a Pakistan chase that requires a very good innings from two or three batsmen and a Pakistan chase that requires a very good innings from two or three batsmen followed by precisely the kind of late drama that Pakistan, as a batting unit, have been producing with unpredictable but unmistakable frequency throughout this tournament.

"When I got out, we had seventy balls to go. As a batter, you want to bat fifty overs. We should've been around three-forty, three-fifty. Credit to their bowlers — Amir was exceptional."

David Warner, Player of the Match, post-match at Taunton

Pakistan's Chase: Structure, Then Collapse, Then Redemption Too Late

Pakistan began their reply with the specific fragility of a batting order that, across this tournament, has shown itself capable of the highest quality and the most sudden disintegration within the span of the same match. Fakhar Zaman — whose form had been the subject of debate even before the tournament began, his place in the top order predicated on the explosive potential that the first fifteen overs over of an ODI innings occasionally reward and just as occasionally destroy — upper-cut the third delivery he received from Pat Cummins to Kane Richardson at third man for a duck. The dismissal carried within it the particular quality of the high-risk opening stroke: the decision to go after the first ball of the innings from a fast bowler in the third over, the calculation that the risk of failure is worth the potential reward of a boundary that sets the tone, and the outcome — here, a catch at third man — that renders the calculation visible as an error in retrospect without quite qualifying as one in the logic of the batsman who made it.

Babar Azam made thirty in the kind of innings that his batting at this World Cup has repeatedly offered: compact, organized, finding the boundary through the off side with the wristiness and placement that makes his cover-driving among the cleanest in the current game, and then departing to a pull shot off Nathan Coulter-Nile that found the fielder at deep backward square leg rather than the gap Babar had imagined. He had scored twenty-eight of his thirty runs in boundaries — the ratio of a batsman who is hitting the ball cleanly and placing it precisely and who has, in this match, been denied the platform that his method requires before it can fully function. Imam-ul-Haq's fifty-three from seventy-five deliveries was the innings that gave the chase its structural coherence: composed, rotational, working the ball through the leg side with the left-hander's natural ease and running between the wickets with the alert efficiency of a batsman who has identified, accurately, that the required rate is manageable if the wickets are preserved and the singles are taken. He was eventually caught behind off Cummins in the twenty-fifth over, gloved down the leg side — the wicket that broke open the Pakistan chase.

Mohammad Hafeez's forty-six from forty-nine deliveries — the innings of a senior batsman playing with the freedom of a man who has recognised the situation and responded with the attacking intent it demanded — had taken Pakistan to one hundred and thirty-six for two in the twenty-sixth over, a position of genuine competitiveness that the scoreboard's arithmetic rendered entirely believable as a launching pad for a successful chase. And then came the collapse. Four wickets fell for twenty-four runs: Hafeez hitting a rank full toss from Aaron Finch's occasional off-spin straight to Starc at deep midwicket — the kind of dismissal that defies rational explanation and requires instead the simple acknowledgment that cricket, in its lower registers, occasionally removes good batsmen for reasons that have nothing to do with the quality of the delivery; Shoaib Malik caught brilliantly by Alex Carey diving forward off the inside edge for a second-ball duck; Asif Ali stumped for five; Sarfaraz Ahmed — their captain, their best chance of orchestrating the recovery — falling in the thirty-third over for thirty-nine, a wicket that, in retrospect, ended the match as a genuine contest.

Wahab and Hasan: The Rearguard That Came Too Late

What Wahab Riaz produced after the collapse — forty-five from thirty-nine deliveries, three sixes and two fours in a cameo of uninhibited, joyful hitting that briefly, and without realistic foundation, made the match feel like something other than the concluded affair the scoreboard insisted it was — was the innings that this Pakistan team has been producing throughout this tournament at the wrong end of matches they have lost. Wahab hits the ball with a left-hander's natural flat trajectory, the pull and the slog-sweep finding the short boundaries at Taunton with a frequency that the fielding positions could not entirely account for. Hasan Ali's thirty-two from twenty-four balls alongside him — two sixes over long-on, struck with a bowler's contempt for the niceties of technical correctness — extended the partnership to sixty-one and reduced the required rate, in a brief window of false promise, to something that the mathematical mind could construct a case for. But when Sarfaraz was dismissed in the thirty-third over — caught by Carey behind the stumps off a Starc delivery, an Australian review upheld that the on-field umpire had declined — the chase was functionally over. Wahab fell in the forty-fourth over, also to Starc. Glenn Maxwell finished the match with a direct-hit run-out of Sarfaraz from mid-on — the kind of athletic intervention that Australia's fielding routinely produces and that, here, was less a decisive moment than the punctuation mark on a sentence already fully written.

There is a question that attaches itself to both teams after this match and that the tournament's remaining group stage games will answer rather than this one. For Australia: whether the depth of batting that Amir's five-for exposed — the six wickets for thirty that converted a probable three-fifty into a three-hundred-and-seven — represents a structural vulnerability or a temporary aberration in a line-up that contains, from one to seven, more than enough quality to post totals of genuine menace. For Pakistan: whether the collapse from one-hundred-and-thirty-six for two to one-hundred-and-sixty for six is the recurrent pattern that opponents have identified and begun to plan around, or whether it is, as Pakistani cricket has always insisted about itself, simply one of those sequences that this team produces before producing the remarkable recovery that makes its campaign worth watching. The forty-one-run margin at Taunton did not answer either question. It merely clarified the terms of the inquiry.
