Steve Smith is the most technically perfect batsman I have seen in thirty years of covering this sport.

The technique is unconventional. The average is sixty-one. The question is not whether he is the best batsman of his generation — it is whether his generation has produced anyone who will be remembered alongside him in the company of the genuinely great.

By Muhammad Hamza Chief Correspondent, The Pitch Report The Pitch Report

The first thing to establish, because it is the objection most commonly raised in this conversation before the conversation has properly begun, is that unconventional technique and technical perfection are not mutually exclusive. They may in fact be the same thing, for a given batsman, in a given context. Steve Smith's technique — the shuffle across the crease, the back-and-across movement that takes him so far to the off side that his off stump is often visible to the bowler, the high backlift that creates a loop in the downswing, the hands held well away from the body at the point of contact — is not the technique that cricket coaches teach. It is not the technique that the MCC Coaching Manual recommends. It is not, to put it plainly, the technique that produces the straight bat, the high elbow, the weight transfer onto the front foot that is the orthodoxy of English batting instruction and has been since the early twentieth century. What it is, however, is the technique that allows Steve Smith to average sixty-one across a hundred Test matches against the best bowling attacks in the world. And if that is not technical perfection, I am not sure what technical perfection is supposed to be.

The argument for Smith begins with the numbers, because the numbers are so large and so sustained that to dismiss them requires a theory of their inflation that nobody making the objection can satisfactorily provide. He averages over sixty. He averages over sixty in England. He averages over sixty in South Africa. He averages over eighty in Australia, where the pitches offer the ball-seam combination that typically disciplines the top-order batsman most severely. He has made thirty Test centuries. He has been dismissed for a duck nine times in a hundred and ten Tests — a frequency that suggests a batsman who almost never gives his wicket away cheaply, who arrives at the crease with the understanding that his job is to be there at the end of the day, and who accomplishes that understanding with a consistency that the dismissal record makes visible. These are not the numbers of a batsman who has been fortunate with the schedule or the conditions. They are the numbers of a batsman who has solved the problem of Test cricket batting more completely than anyone since Sachin Tendulkar.

The Problem He Is Solving

To understand what Smith is doing technically, you need to understand what problem he is solving. The fundamental problem of Test match batting against world-class seam bowling is the delivery that hits the top of off stump or takes the outside edge into the slips cordon — the delivery that exploits the gap between bat and pad, or finds the outside edge of the bat that is reaching for the ball on the off side. This is the delivery that dismisses most top-order batsmen at the highest level, and it is the delivery against which the coaching orthodoxy's front-foot technique is most vulnerable, because the front-foot drive requires committing the weight forward and presenting the outside edge of the bat in a position that the ball moving away late can exploit. Smith's back-and-across movement removes this vulnerability. By moving his weight onto the back foot before the ball is bowled, he gives himself the time — a fraction of a second, but a fraction that matters enormously at this level — to see the ball move off the seam and adjust his shot accordingly. His backlift, which most coaches seeing it for the first time describe as a problem to be corrected, provides the same function: the loop in the downswing creates additional time at the crucial moment of ball contact, allowing Smith to make micro-adjustments to his shot after the ball has pitched that a conventional straight-back-and-forward swing does not permit.

This is not an accident. Smith has spoken, in interviews and in his autobiography, about the deliberateness with which he developed these techniques — not as a rejection of coaching orthodoxy but as a response to the specific problems that world-class bowling presented to him in Test matches, problems that the orthodox techniques he was taught as a junior could not solve. The result is a technique that looks unorthodox because it is — it was designed by the batsman for the batsman, around the specific demands of the highest level of the game — but that is, in the most meaningful sense of the word, technically superior to the orthodox alternatives, because it produces better results against the best bowling in the world over the longest available duration.

When the Ashes come round, when the pitches are green and the Duke ball is swinging and the best seamers in England are running in on mornings that were designed to produce their dismissal, Smith bats as though the conditions are irrelevant. That is not confidence. That is mastery.

The Ashes of 2019: The Evidence That Cannot Be Dismissed

The case for Smith is made most completely by the Ashes series of 2019, and specifically by the fact that England — with Jofra Archer bowling at ninety-two miles per hour, with Stuart Broad in full flight at Headingley and Lord's and Old Trafford, with conditions prepared specifically to exploit the outside edge of a right-handed batsman — could not get him out for fewer than sixty on the occasions when he batted, except through the intervention of a bouncer from Archer at Lord's that struck him on the neck and concussed him out of the match. Smith made seven hundred and seventy-four runs in that series, across seven innings, at an average of a hundred and ten. He made a hundred and forty-four and a hundred and forty-two at Edgbaston. He made a hundred and eleven at Manchester. He made eighty-two at The Oval. The England bowling attack — which had, that same summer, bowled Australia out for sixty at Headingley and two hundred and twenty-three in the semi-final of the World Cup — was comprehensively insufficient against one batsman. Not defeated in a single great innings but defeated systematically, across twenty-one sessions of Test cricket, by a man who appeared to have already solved every problem that they were capable of posing.

The comparison with Tendulkar is the one that serious cricket people make when they try to locate Smith in the historical record, and it is the right comparison because it is the comparison that Smith's own numbers suggest rather than the one being imposed by sentiment. Tendulkar averages fifty-three point seven across two hundred and forty-four Tests. Smith, at the time of writing, averages sixty-one point eight across a hundred and ten. The difference is partially explained by era — Test cricket in Tendulkar's time involved a wider range of conditions and opponents, including more series against high-quality bowling attacks in unfamiliar conditions — and partially by the simple fact that Tendulkar played for twenty-four years and that the final years of any great career produce numbers that dilute the average of the peak. Smith, if he plays for ten more years, will face the same dilution. What cannot be diluted is the peak, and Smith's peak — from 2014 to 2019, and then again in 2023 and 2024 — is among the most sustained in the game's history.

The Aesthetic Objection and Why It Matters Less Than You Think

The objection most frequently made to the claim that Smith is the greatest batsman of his era, and possibly of his generation, is the aesthetic one: that his batting is ungainly, that watching him bat lacks the visual pleasure of watching, say, Kumar Sangakkara drive through the covers or Michael Vaughan cut behind point, that there is something uncomfortable about the way his body organises itself at the crease that makes watching him accumulate his hundreds a slightly unsatisfying experience. This objection is real — it is not imagined, and the people making it are not simply being contrarian — but it is irrelevant to the question of technical perfection. Technical perfection, in sport as in engineering, is not about elegance. It is about function. The technique that performs the function of allowing its user to score runs at the highest level against the best bowling in the most demanding conditions, over the longest duration, more effectively than any alternative technique is, by definition, technically perfect. That it does not look like the techniques we were taught to admire is our problem, not Smith's.

There is also, and this is worth noting, a gradual aesthetic accommodation happening in the cricket watching public's relationship with Smith's batting. The people who have watched him for a decade — who have seen, over many series and many sessions, the consistency and authority with which that unusual technique produces run after run in situations that other batsmen find too technically demanding — have developed, most of them, a particular appreciation for what they are watching. It does not look like Lara's cover drive. It does not look like Ponting's pull shot. What it looks like is a craftsman who has thought harder than anyone else about the specific problems of his profession and produced a solution that is uniquely and completely his own. That is, in the end, what technical perfection in sport looks like. Not the received forms, but the solution that works — for this batsman, in these conditions, against this bowling, at this level. Smith works. He has worked for a decade, against every bowling attack the world has produced, in every condition the game offers. I have been watching cricket for thirty years. I have not seen a better Test batsman. I do not expect to see one for a considerable time.
